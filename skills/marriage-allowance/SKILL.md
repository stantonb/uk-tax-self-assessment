## What Is Marriage Allowance?

Marriage Allowance lets one partner transfer a fixed portion of their Personal Allowance to the other. See `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/references/2024-25-rates.md` for the exact transferable amount and maximum annual saving.

## Eligibility

Both of the following must apply:

- The couple is **married or in a civil partnership**.
- One partner (the transferor) has income below their Personal Allowance — they are a non-taxpayer or earn below the PA threshold in the rates file.
- The other partner (the recipient) is a **basic rate taxpayer** — their income must not exceed the higher rate threshold in the rates file.

The transfer is one-way: the lower earner transfers to the higher earner, not the reverse.

## How It Works

The transferor gives up a slice of their Personal Allowance (see rates file for the amount). Their PA decreases by that amount. The recipient receives a tax reduction equal to that amount multiplied by the basic rate of tax.

This is applied as a **tax reduction**, not an increase to the recipient's Personal Allowance. The distinction matters: it reduces the tax bill directly rather than increasing the tax-free income band.

## Maximum Saving

The maximum saving per year is stated in the rates file. It equals the transferred amount multiplied by the basic rate. The saving goes to the recipient, not the transferor.

## How to Claim

- **Online** — Apply via the HMRC Marriage Allowance service at gov.uk. The transferor must apply.
- **Via Self Assessment** — The recipient can claim on their SA return. The transferor can also cancel through their SA return if circumstances change.

Once set up, Marriage Allowance renews automatically each year until cancelled.

## Backdating Claims

Claims can be **backdated up to 4 years**. Any backdated refund is paid to the recipient as a lump sum or adjustment to their tax bill. This can result in a meaningful refund covering multiple years.

## When Marriage Allowance Is NOT Beneficial

Do not claim Marriage Allowance if:

- The **recipient is a higher or additional rate taxpayer** — they are ineligible.
- The **transferor has taxable income** that would become taxable after losing part of their PA — the lost allowance may generate a tax charge exceeding the recipient's saving.
- The transferor has **savings income** that, combined with the reduced PA, would push them into a taxable position. The interaction between the Personal Savings Allowance and reduced PA needs checking.

## Interaction with Savings Income

Reducing the transferor's PA means more of their income is taxable. If the transferor has savings income that currently falls within their PA, losing part of it could:

- Push savings income into the basic rate band.
- Use up the Personal Savings Allowance sooner.

Always check whether the transferor's total income (including savings interest) stays below their reduced PA before applying.

## Difference from Married Couple's Allowance

Marriage Allowance is **not** the same as the old Married Couple's Allowance (MCA). The MCA is a separate, more valuable relief available only where one partner was **born before 6 April 1935**. Key differences:

- MCA provides a higher tax reduction (see rates file if applicable).
- MCA is available to higher rate taxpayers.
- A couple **cannot claim both** Marriage Allowance and MCA.

If either partner was born before 6 April 1935, check whether MCA gives a better result before opting for Marriage Allowance.

## Practical Points

- Marriage Allowance applies for the **whole tax year** — it cannot be split part-year if circumstances change mid-year (e.g., the recipient becomes a higher rate taxpayer partway through).
- If one partner dies, the surviving partner can still claim for the year of death.
- Divorce or dissolution ends the allowance from the next tax year.
