## 2. Bitcoin Capital Gain (£4,500)

### How HMRC Treats Cryptocurrency

HMRC treats Bitcoin and all other cryptoassets as **property**, not currency. Selling your Bitcoin for a gain is a **disposal** subject to Capital Gains Tax (CGT) — not income tax.

### The Annual Exempt Amount

For 2024/25, the CGT Annual Exempt Amount is **£3,000**. This means only the gain **above £3,000** is taxable.

- **Your gain**: £4,500
- **Less Annual Exempt Amount**: £3,000
- **Taxable gain**: **£1,500**

### CGT Rate — Important Mid-Year Change

For 2024/25, CGT rates on most assets (including crypto) changed on **30 October 2024**:

| Period | Basic rate taxpayer | Higher rate taxpayer |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 6 Apr 2024 – 29 Oct 2024 | 10% | 20% |
| 30 Oct 2024 – 5 Apr 2025 | 18% | 24% |

At £72,000, your income already exceeds the basic rate band (£50,270). This means you are a **higher rate taxpayer** for CGT purposes, and your gain sits on top of your income. The CGT rate that applies depends on **when** you sold the Bitcoin:

- If you sold **before 30 October 2024**: the rate is **20%** on the taxable gain
- If you sold **on or after 30 October 2024**: the rate is **24%** on the taxable gain

**CGT due on Bitcoin gain**:
- At 20% (pre-30 Oct): £1,500 × 20% = **£300**
- At 24% (post-30 Oct): £1,500 × 24% = **£360**

If your disposal straddled the date, you would need to apportion. Please confirm the sale date.

### Cost Basis and Share Pooling

When you calculated your £4,500 gain, make sure you applied HMRC's share pooling rules in the correct order:
1. Same-day rule — match against any Bitcoin bought on the same day as the sale
2. 30-day bed and breakfast rule — match against any Bitcoin bought within 30 days after the sale
3. Section 104 pool — match against the pooled average cost of all remaining Bitcoin

If you bought and sold Bitcoin at different times, the cost basis must reflect the pooled average, not necessarily the specific coins you "think" you sold.

### Record-Keeping

Keep records of every transaction: date, amount in Bitcoin, GBP value at the time, exchange used, and any fees. HMRC can request these records — retain them for at least **5 years** after the 31 January filing deadline.

### Reporting

You will report this gain on the **SA108 Capital Gains Tax summary pages** of your Self Assessment return. Even though part of your gain is covered by the Annual Exempt Amount, you must still report the disposal.

## 3. SIP Shares — The 3-Year Point (Critical Warning)

This is arguably the most important section for you to read carefully.

### The 3-to-5-Year Trap — You Are In It Right Now

The tax treatment of SIP shares on withdrawal is governed by a three-tier holding period:

| Holding period | Income Tax and NI liability |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| Less than 3 years | Full market value at withdrawal taxed as employment income |
| **3 to 5 years** | **Lower of market value at award OR market value at withdrawal — taxed as employment income** |
| 5 years or more | No Income Tax or NI at all — complete tax relief |

You have held your shares for **3 years**. This puts you squarely in the **middle tier**. You are **not** in the tax-free zone — that only begins at the **5-year mark**.

### What This Means Practically

If you sell now, Income Tax and NI will be charged on the **lower of**:
- The market value of the shares when they were **awarded** to you, or
- The market value of the shares **when you withdraw** them

**Example**: Suppose your shares were worth £5,000 when awarded and are now worth £8,000. The taxable amount is £5,000 (the lower figure). That £5,000 is treated as employment income and taxed at your marginal rate. At £72,000, your marginal rate is **40%** (plus NI at **2%** above the Upper Earnings Limit of £50,270). The combined income tax and NI cost on that £5,000 would be approximately £2,100.

Conversely, if your shares have fallen in value to £3,000, the taxable amount is £3,000 — you still pay tax, just on a smaller amount.

**Either way, there is a tax charge. This is a common misconception — many employees believe 3 years is the tax-free milestone. It is not.**

### The Recommendation

If you can afford to wait, **hold your SIP shares until the 5-year anniversary**. At that point, no Income Tax or NI will be due on withdrawal whatsoever. That could represent a saving of thousands of pounds depending on the share value.

### What Happens After Withdrawal

Once you withdraw shares from the SIP trust (at any point), they enter your personal ownership. Any **further gain** from the withdrawal date onwards is subject to Capital Gains Tax when you eventually sell. The CGT base cost is the market value at the date of withdrawal. Pair this with your Annual Exempt Amount (£3,000 for 2024/25) to plan future disposals tax-efficiently.

### Employer Handling

If you do proceed with withdrawal before 5 years, your employer should handle the Income Tax and NI through **PAYE**. Verify this appears correctly on your payslip or P11D — it should not fall through the cracks and land unexpectedly on your SA return.

## 4. Plan 2 Student Loan

### Your Repayment Threshold

For 2024/25, Plan 2 repayments are calculated as:

At £72,000:
- Income above threshold: £72,000 − £27,295 = **£44,705**
- Annual repayment due: £44,705 × 9% = **£4,023**

### PAYE Already Handles This

Your employer will deduct Plan 2 student loan repayments automatically through PAYE, based on a Student Loan Notice (SL1) from HMRC. You should see this as a separate deduction on your payslip.

### Self Assessment and Additional Repayments

On your Self Assessment return, you will confirm your student loan plan type. HMRC will calculate the total repayment due on your **total income** (including the Bitcoin capital gain — though note that capital gains are generally not included in the student loan repayment calculation, which is based on income, not gains).

For this year, your SA return simply needs to reflect the Plan 2 deductions already made via PAYE. Provided your employer has deducted correctly throughout the year, there should be no surprise additional repayment due through SA.

### Check Your Payslips

Confirm that student loan deductions appear on your payslips. If they are missing (which can happen after starting a new job, for example), HMRC may collect the shortfall through your SA balancing payment on **31 January 2026**.
