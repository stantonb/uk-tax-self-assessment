## Do You Need to Do a Self Assessment?

**Yes, you almost certainly do need to register for Self Assessment.** Here's why:

HMRC requires you to complete a Self Assessment tax return if you have dividend income over £1,000 in a tax year (for 2023/24 onwards, the dividend allowance was reduced to £1,000; from 2024/25 it remains £1,000). Your dividends of £2,300 exceed the £1,000 dividend allowance, meaning the excess is taxable — and HMRC's general rule is that if you have any taxable dividend income not already taxed at source, you must notify HMRC and complete a return.

Additionally, if your total income exceeds £10,000 from savings and investments combined, or you have untaxed income, Self Assessment may be required. In your case, the dividend trigger alone is sufficient.

## Tax Calculation

### Step 1: Personal Allowance

The standard Personal Allowance for 2025/26 is **£12,570**.

Your pension of £14,000 exceeds this, so your pension uses up the full Personal Allowance:

- Pension income: £14,000
- Personal Allowance used against pension: £12,570
- Pension taxable at basic rate: £14,000 - £12,570 = **£1,430** (taxed via PAYE at 20% = £286, already deducted by pension provider)

This leaves no Personal Allowance remaining for savings or dividends.

### Step 2: Savings Interest — £1,800

Since all your Personal Allowance is used by the pension, we check the savings nil-rate bands:

- **Starting Rate for Savings**: Up to £5,000 at 0%, BUT this is only available if your non-savings income (pension) does not exceed Personal Allowance + £5,000. Your pension is £14,000, which is £1,430 above the Personal Allowance. This means the Starting Rate band is reduced by £1,430, leaving: £5,000 - £1,430 = **£3,570** still available at 0%.
- Your savings interest of £1,800 is fully within this £3,570 band.
- **Personal Savings Allowance (PSA)**: Basic rate taxpayers get £1,000 of savings interest tax-free. However, since your £1,800 is already covered by the Starting Rate for Savings, the PSA isn't additionally needed here.

**Savings tax owed: £0**

### Step 3: Dividends — £2,300

- **Dividend Allowance**: £500 (this was reduced from £1,000 to £500 from April 2024, i.e., the 2024/25 tax year onwards)
- Taxable dividends: £2,300 - £500 = **£1,800**
- These fall within the basic rate band
- **Dividend basic rate tax: 8.75%**
- Tax on dividends: £1,800 × 8.75% = **£157.50**

## Key Points to Note

1. **Register for Self Assessment** by 5 October following the end of the tax year in which you had the taxable dividends. For 2025/26 income, that means registering by 5 October 2026.

2. **Filing deadlines**: Paper return by 31 October; online return by 31 January following the tax year end.

3. **Your pension tax**: Your pension provider should already be deducting tax via PAYE on the £1,430 taxable portion (~£286/year). This does not need to be paid again — Self Assessment reconciles everything.

4. **ISA note**: Any savings interest or dividends within an ISA are completely tax-free and don't count toward any of the above figures or allowances.

5. **Check your tax code**: HMRC may adjust your pension tax code to collect the dividend tax automatically in future years, meaning you might not need to file a return every year — but you must notify them initially.

## Bottom Line

You owe approximately **£157.50 in extra tax**, purely on your dividends. You should register for Self Assessment to declare this.
