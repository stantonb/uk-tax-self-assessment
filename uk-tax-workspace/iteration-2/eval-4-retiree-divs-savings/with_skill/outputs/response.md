## Part 1: Do You Need to File a Self Assessment Return?

**Yes, you almost certainly need to file a Self Assessment return.**

The key triggers that apply to you are:

1. **Dividends above the Dividend Allowance** — you receive £2,300 in dividends, but the 2024/25 Dividend Allowance is only £500. The £1,800 excess is taxable and must be reported via Self Assessment.
2. **Savings interest above the Personal Savings Allowance (PSA)** — as we will establish below, you are a basic rate taxpayer, so your PSA is £1,000. Your savings interest of £1,800 exceeds this by £800, which is potentially taxable. However, as explained in Part 2, the Starting Rate for Savings may eliminate the tax on this interest entirely. Nonetheless, you still need to file because of the dividend position.

HMRC lists "dividends or savings interest above allowances" as an explicit Self Assessment filing trigger. Even if no tax is ultimately owed, if you have income above these allowances you are required to file.

**Action required**: If you are not already registered for Self Assessment, you need to register by **5 October 2025** (for the 2024/25 tax year). The online return deadline is **31 January 2026**, with any tax due payable on the same date.

## Part 2: How Your Income Is Taxed — Step-by-Step

UK income tax applies a strict stacking order:
1. Non-savings income (pension) — taxed first
2. Savings income (bank/building society interest) — taxed next
3. Dividend income — taxed last, sitting on top of everything else

### Step 1 — Establish Your Tax Band

Your pension of £14,000 is non-savings income. After deducting the Personal Allowance of £12,570, your taxable non-savings income is:

```
£14,000 − £12,570 = £1,430 taxable at basic rate (20%)
```

You are comfortably a **basic rate taxpayer**. Your total income of £18,100 is well below the higher rate threshold of £50,270.

### Step 2 — Assess the Starting Rate for Savings

This is a valuable and often overlooked allowance, particularly for retirees with modest pension income.

The Starting Rate for Savings provides a 0% band of up to **£5,000** on savings interest. However, it is reduced by £1 for every £1 of non-savings income that exceeds the Personal Allowance.

```
Non-savings income above the Personal Allowance:
£14,000 − £12,570 = £1,430

Starting Rate for Savings available:
£5,000 − £1,430 = £3,570 at 0%
```

You have **£3,570 of starting rate band available** — more than enough to cover your entire £1,800 of savings interest.

### Step 3 — Apply the Personal Savings Allowance (PSA)

As a basic rate taxpayer, your PSA is **£1,000** at 0%.

However, because your savings interest of £1,800 is entirely covered by the £3,570 Starting Rate band (which takes priority), the PSA is not actually needed here — but it would provide further shelter if your savings interest were higher.

**Result: No tax is due on your savings interest.** The entire £1,800 falls within the Starting Rate for Savings band at 0%.

### Step 4 — Tax the Dividend Income

Dividends are stacked on top of all other income. To determine which tax band they fall into, we need to see how much of the basic rate band has been used up.

```
Basic rate band:              £12,571 – £50,270  (width: £37,700)

Used by:
  Pension above allowance:    £1,430
  Savings interest:           £1,800
  (both consume the basic rate band, even at 0%)

Total band used:              £3,230

Remaining basic rate band:    £37,700 − £3,230 = £34,470
```

Your £2,300 of dividends fits entirely within the remaining basic rate band.

Now apply the Dividend Allowance of **£500** (at 0%), then tax the rest at the basic dividend rate of **8.75%**:

```
Dividend Allowance (0%):      £500   → £0 tax
Taxable dividends (8.75%):    £1,800 → £157.50 tax
                              (£2,300 − £500 = £1,800)
```

### How Much Extra Tax Do You Owe?

This depends on what, if anything, is already being collected:

- **Pension income tax (£286.00)**: If your pension provider operates PAYE, this tax is likely already being deducted at source through your tax code. Check your pension payslips or P60. If tax has already been collected on your pension, this portion is not an additional liability.
- **Tax on savings interest (£0)**: No additional tax is due. Banks pay interest gross since April 2016 — no deduction happens automatically — but in your case no tax is actually owed on the interest thanks to the Starting Rate for Savings.
- **Tax on dividends (£157.50)**: This is almost certainly not being collected anywhere. There is no automatic withholding on UK dividends. **This £157.50 is the most likely amount you will owe as a result of filing your Self Assessment return.**

**If your pension is already being taxed correctly via PAYE, your additional tax liability through Self Assessment is approximately £157.50.**

## Part 4: Key Points to Note

**Starting Rate for Savings is very valuable for you.** Because your pension is only slightly above the Personal Allowance, you retain a large chunk of this 0% band. Many retirees in your position are unaware of it and worry unnecessarily about paying tax on savings interest. In your case, the entire £1,800 is sheltered.

**The Dividend Allowance has fallen sharply.** It was £5,000 in 2017/18, reduced to £2,000 in 2018/19, then cut to £1,000 in 2023/24, and cut again to £500 in 2024/25. If your dividend income grows, the tax due will increase accordingly. Consider whether holding dividend-paying shares within an ISA would be beneficial — ISA dividends are completely tax-free and do not need to be reported.

**Your savings interest position could change.** If interest rates remain high and your savings balances grow, you could exceed the Starting Rate band. At £5,000 of savings interest, the Starting Rate would be exhausted and your PSA would begin absorbing further interest. Above £6,800 of savings interest (£3,570 starting rate + £1,000 PSA + the £2,230 already covered by the starting rate... actually at interest above £4,570 the PSA kicks in, and above £5,570 you would pay tax), you would begin paying 20% on the excess.

**National Insurance**: As a retired person receiving a pension, you do not pay National Insurance on pension income. NI is not a concern here.

## Part 5: What You Need to Do

1. **Register for Self Assessment** if not already registered — by 5 October 2025 for 2024/25.
2. **Gather your documents**:
   - Pension P60 (showing gross pension and any tax deducted)
   - Interest statements from all non-ISA savings accounts (annual summaries)
   - Broker statements or dividend vouchers showing total dividends received
3. **File your SA100 online by 31 January 2026** — include pension income, savings interest (in the savings section), and dividends (in the dividends section).
4. **Pay any tax due by 31 January 2026** — approximately £157.50 if your pension tax is already being handled by PAYE.
5. **Consider ISA transfers** — moving dividend-paying shares into a Stocks and Shares ISA would remove them from the taxable calculation entirely in future years, eliminating the £157.50 annual tax liability.
