# Your Savings Interest and Self Assessment — 2024/25 Tax Year

Your situation involves several savings accounts and a common misconception about how banks handle tax. Let me work through each element carefully.

---

## First: The Key Misconception About Banks and Tax

You mentioned thinking "banks sorted out the tax automatically." This used to be the case, but it changed in April 2016. Since then, banks and building societies pay all interest **gross** — no tax is deducted at source. Your bank does report your interest directly to HMRC, but the responsibility for ensuring any tax due is actually paid sits entirely with you, either through Self Assessment or via HMRC adjusting your PAYE tax code.

## The Starting Rate for Savings — Does It Apply?

There is a 0% starting rate band for savings income of up to £5,000, but it is only available to the extent that your non-savings income does not fill it. The calculation is:

- Non-savings income: £48,000
- Less Personal Allowance: £12,570
- Taxable non-savings income: £35,430

The starting rate band (£5,000) is only available if taxable non-savings income is below £5,000. Your taxable non-savings income of £35,430 far exceeds this, so **the starting rate for savings does not apply to you**. This relief primarily benefits retirees or those with very low earned income.

## Do You Need to File a Self Assessment Return?

Your savings interest of £1,600 exceeds your PSA of £1,000, leaving £600 of untaxed taxable interest. Whether you need to file a Self Assessment return depends on how HMRC handles this:

**Option 1 — HMRC adjusts your PAYE tax code**: HMRC receives interest data directly from your banks and NS&I. For relatively modest amounts like £600 of taxable savings interest, HMRC will often collect the tax by reducing your PAYE tax code in a future year rather than requiring a full Self Assessment return. You may receive a "Simple Assessment" letter instead.

**Option 2 — Self Assessment is required**: You would need to file a Self Assessment return if:
- HMRC issues you a formal notice to file (SA316)
- You have other income sources that already require SA (rental income, self-employment, etc.)
- The amount cannot be collected via a PAYE code adjustment
- You choose to file voluntarily to manage your tax affairs

Given that your only apparent trigger is savings interest above the PSA, and the excess amount is relatively modest (£600 of additional taxable interest, £120 of tax), HMRC is likely to handle this via a code adjustment or Simple Assessment rather than requiring a full Self Assessment return. However, if you have not heard from HMRC and believe you owe tax, you should contact HMRC or register for Self Assessment to ensure it is paid.

**If you are required to file (or already file)**: the online return deadline for the 2024/25 tax year is **31 January 2026**, with any balancing payment also due on that date.

## Practical Steps to Take

1. **Gather interest certificates**: Obtain annual statements or tax certificates from Marcus, Nationwide, and NS&I for the 2024/25 tax year (6 April 2024 to 5 April 2025). Banks typically issue these in April/May.
2. **Exclude Premium Bond prizes**: Do not include the £500 anywhere on a tax return — it is exempt.
3. **Check for HMRC correspondence**: HMRC may write to you with a Simple Assessment or a revised tax code. Review any letters carefully.
4. **Register for Self Assessment if needed**: If you receive a notice to file (SA316), or if you have other reasons to file a return, register with HMRC by **5 October 2025** for the 2024/25 tax year.
5. **Keep records**: Retain your interest statements for at least 5 years after the 31 January 2026 submission deadline.
