# Your Tax Position at £112,000 — and What You Can Do About It

Your accountant friend is absolutely right, and the situation is actually even more complex than just the "60% trap" — you have three overlapping issues hitting you at once. Let's take each one apart and then show you how pension contributions can solve most of them in one move.

---

## Issue 1: The Personal Allowance Trap (the "60% band")

This is real. Here is the mechanism:

- Everyone gets a Personal Allowance of **£12,570** — income you pay zero tax on.
- But once your **adjusted net income (ANI)** exceeds **£100,000**, you lose **£1 of that allowance for every £2 earned above £100,000**.
- At £125,140 your Personal Allowance is gone entirely.

At £112,000, you are **£12,000 into this taper zone**.

**Why it creates a ~60% effective rate:**

On each extra £2 you earn above £100,000:
- You pay 40% income tax on those £2 = **80p of tax**
- You also lose £1 of Personal Allowance — which means £1 of previously tax-free income is now taxed at 40% = another **40p of tax**
- Total tax on £2 of extra income = **£1.20**, i.e. **60%**

**Your current exposure:**

You are £12,000 above the £100,000 threshold, so £12,000 of your income is in this 60% effective rate band. That costs you roughly **£2,400 more in tax** compared with what you would pay if the taper did not exist (i.e. compared with just paying 40% on that £12,000).

At your current salary of £112,000, your Personal Allowance is reduced to:
- £12,570 − (£12,000 / 2) = **£6,570** (reduced by £6,000)

## Issue 2: The High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC)

You receive Child Benefit for 3 children. For 2024/25, the weekly rates are:
- Eldest child: **£25.60/week**
- Each additional child: **£16.95/week**

Annual Child Benefit for 3 children:
- £25.60 × 52 = £1,331.20
- £16.95 × 52 × 2 = £1,762.80
- **Total: £3,094.00 per year**

The HICBC claws this back at **1% for every £200** of ANI above **£60,000** (the 2024/25 threshold).

**Your position at ANI of £112,000:**

- You are £52,000 above the £60,000 threshold
- That is 52,000 / 200 = **260 units** of 1%
- But the charge is capped at 100%, so the full £3,094 is clawed back
- The full clawback threshold is £80,000 — you are well past it
- You are repaying **the entire £3,094 in Child Benefit** through the HICBC charge

This is effectively a complete loss of your Child Benefit, and it is collected via Self Assessment — which means you **must file a Self Assessment return** if you are not already doing so.

## The Solution: Increase Your Pension Contributions via Salary Sacrifice

This is where it all comes together. Your workplace pension already uses **salary sacrifice** — this is the most tax-efficient mechanism available to you. Here is why additional contributions are so powerful.

### How Salary Sacrifice Works

With salary sacrifice, your contractual salary is reduced, and the "sacrificed" amount goes directly into your pension as an employer contribution. Because your gross pay is lower:

1. You pay **less income tax** (fewer pounds are taxable)
2. You pay **less employee NI** (lower gross pay = lower NI liability)
3. Your employer pays **less employer NI** (a saving employers sometimes share back)
4. Your **adjusted net income** falls — which is the key figure for both the Personal Allowance taper and the HICBC

### Current Pension Contributions

Currently you and your employer each contribute 5%:
- Your 5% = £5,600/year (salary sacrifice, so already reducing your gross pay)
- Employer 5% = £5,600/year
- **Total going in: £11,200/year**

Your ANI is approximately £112,000 − £5,600 (your salary sacrifice) = **£106,400**

Wait — this matters. If your current 5% salary sacrifice is already being deducted, your ANI may already be £106,400, not £112,000. Let's work with both figures:

**Scenario A: ANI currently = £106,400 (salary sacrifice already applied)**
- Still £6,400 above £100,000 — in the 60% trap for £6,400
- Still well above £80,000 — full HICBC applies

**Scenario B: ANI = £112,000 (contributions handled differently)**
- £12,000 above £100,000

Either way, additional pension contributions can fix both problems.

### Target: Bring ANI to £60,000

To eliminate *both* the Personal Allowance trap *and* the entire HICBC charge, you would need to bring ANI down to **£60,000**.

From a starting ANI of £106,400 (Scenario A), that requires an **additional £46,400** of salary sacrifice contributions.

From £112,000 (Scenario B), that requires an additional **£52,000** of contributions.

That may be more than you want to contribute. Let's look at the more achievable targets.

### Target 1: Restore the Full Personal Allowance (bring ANI to £100,000)

**Starting from ANI of £106,400:**
- Additional contributions needed: **£6,400**
- This restores **£3,200 of Personal Allowance** (you get back £1 for every £2 reduced)
- Wait — actually, to restore the *full* personal allowance you need to bring ANI to exactly £100,000
- Additional contributions needed: £6,400
- Tax saving on those £6,400: at an effective 60% marginal rate = **£3,840 saved in income tax**
- NI saving at 2% (you are above the UEL): £6,400 × 2% = **£128**
- **Total saving: approximately £3,968 on a £6,400 contribution**

That is a return of about **62%** on money that goes into your pension — not lost, just locked away until retirement.

### Target 2: Escape the HICBC entirely (bring ANI to £60,000)

To stop paying back the £3,094 in Child Benefit, you need ANI at or below £60,000. From £106,400, that means an additional **£46,400** in salary sacrifice.

On top of the Personal Allowance savings, the additional contributions between £100,000 and £60,000 attract:
- 40% income tax relief
- 2% NI relief (above UEL)
- Recovery of the full HICBC charge worth £3,094

The full HICBC saving of £3,094 on contributions in the £60k-£100k range makes the effective relief on those pounds significantly higher than a straight 40%.

### A Practical Middle Ground

If contributing enough to get ANI to £60,000 is too much for cash flow purposes, consider at minimum:

1. **Contribute enough to get ANI to £100,000** — this fully restores the Personal Allowance and eliminates the 60% trap entirely. The cost is relatively small and the return is exceptional.

2. **Then decide how much further below £100,000 you want to go** — every pound below £100,000 attracts 40% tax relief + 2% NI saving via salary sacrifice.

3. **HICBC reduction** — even getting ANI to, say, £80,000 eliminates the HICBC entirely. From £106,400 that means an extra **£26,400** of contributions.

## Your Wife's Position and Marriage Allowance

Your wife earns **£28,000**. A few points:

**Marriage Allowance:** This allows a lower earner to transfer £1,260 of their Personal Allowance to a basic-rate taxpaying spouse for a saving of £252/year. However, **you are not eligible** — Marriage Allowance is only available where the receiving spouse is a *basic rate* taxpayer (income up to £50,270). At £112,000 you are a higher rate taxpayer, so this does not apply to you.

**HICBC falls on you, not her:** The HICBC charge falls on the higher earner in the household. That is you. Your wife's income of £28,000 is not relevant to the HICBC calculation — it is entirely based on your ANI.

**She claims Child Benefit:** Whoever claims it in your household is fine — the charge falls on whoever has the higher ANI. It is worth making sure she is the one registered to receive Child Benefit since the NI credits (for a parent not working, or if she ever takes a career break) attach to the claimant.

## Self Assessment — You Must File

You are required to file a Self Assessment return because:

1. Your income exceeds the basic PAYE threshold for SA (income over £100,000)
2. You are liable to the HICBC

**Register promptly if you have not already done so.** The deadline to register for Self Assessment for 2024/25 is **5 October 2025**, with the online return due **31 January 2026**.

On your SA return you will:
- Declare your employment income (from your P60)
- Report the Child Benefit received and allow HMRC to calculate the HICBC charge
- Ensure your salary sacrifice contributions are correctly reflected in your ANI

At £112,000, without action, you are in one of the worst positions in the UK tax system — the Personal Allowance taper, HICBC clawback, and higher rate tax all compound against you. But the solution is straightforward: salary sacrifice pension contributions are one of the most tax-efficient things a UK taxpayer in your position can do.
