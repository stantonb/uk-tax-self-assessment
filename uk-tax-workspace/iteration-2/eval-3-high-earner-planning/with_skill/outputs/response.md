# Tax Planning for a £105,000 Earner — 2024/25

> **DISCLAIMER**: This response provides general tax information only. It is NOT professional tax advice. Tax situations vary — always verify with HMRC guidance or a qualified accountant before making decisions. HMRC is the sole authority on UK tax matters.

---

## Your Situation at a Glance

You earn £105,000. Your wife has no income. You have two children and claim Child Benefit. You have access to a SIPP and a salary sacrifice pension scheme through your employer.

Three costly tax problems are stacking up simultaneously:

1. **The personal allowance trap** — your £105,000 income is £5,000 above the £100,000 threshold, meaning you have already lost £2,500 of your personal allowance, creating an effective marginal tax rate of 60% on that £5,000.
2. **The High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC)** — at £105,000 you are well above the £60,000 HICBC threshold, meaning you face a significant clawback of Child Benefit.
3. **Self Assessment obligation** — you are required to file a Self Assessment return because of the HICBC, and possibly also because your income exceeds £100,000.

The good news: pension contributions — especially via salary sacrifice — can solve all three problems at once.

---

## Problem 1: The Personal Allowance Trap (60% Effective Tax Rate)

For 2024/25 the personal allowance is £12,570. For every £2 your adjusted net income (ANI) exceeds £100,000, you lose £1 of personal allowance. The allowance is fully withdrawn at £125,140.

**Your current position at £105,000 ANI:**

- Income above £100,000: £5,000
- Personal allowance lost: £5,000 ÷ 2 = **£2,500**
- Remaining personal allowance: £12,570 − £2,500 = **£10,070**

The £5,000 trapped in the taper zone is effectively taxed at 60%:
- 40% higher rate income tax on the income itself, plus
- 40% tax on the personal allowance lost (£2,500 × 40% = £1,000 extra tax on top of tax already paid on that slice).

To fully escape the trap you need to bring ANI back down to **£100,000 or below**. That means reducing it by **at least £5,000**. Additional pension contributions of £5,000 into your SIPP or via salary sacrifice would achieve this — and at a 60% effective rate, each £1 of contribution costs you only 40p in real terms in this band.

---

## Problem 2: The High Income Child Benefit Charge

From 6 April 2024 the HICBC thresholds are:

- **Charge starts at:** £60,000 ANI
- **Full clawback at:** £80,000 ANI
- **Rate:** 1% of Child Benefit received for every £200 of ANI above £60,000

Child Benefit rates (weekly) for 2024/25:
- Eldest child: £25.60
- Second child: £16.95
- **Total for two children: £42.55/week = approximately £2,213/year**

**Your HICBC calculation at £105,000 ANI:**

- ANI above £60,000 threshold: £45,000
- Number of £200 increments: £45,000 ÷ £200 = 225
- Charge rate: 225 × 1% = **100%** (capped at 100%)
- **HICBC: £2,213** — you are repaying the entire Child Benefit received

At £105,000 you are already above the £80,000 full-clawback ceiling, so you are losing 100% of your Child Benefit. If you reduce ANI to below £80,000 the charge starts falling; below £60,000 it disappears entirely.

**Target to eliminate HICBC entirely: reduce ANI to below £60,000** — that would require £45,000 of pension contributions from your £105,000 income. That is a large step but even reducing ANI to £80,000 (requiring £25,000 of contributions) would start reducing the charge, and every £200 reduction in ANI above £60,000 saves 1% of £2,213 = approximately £22.

## Problem 3: Self Assessment Filing Obligation

You must file a Self Assessment return because:

- Your income exceeds £100,000 (SA filing threshold)
- You are liable for the HICBC

Register with HMRC promptly if you have not already done so. Do not wait for HMRC to contact you — late filing and payment penalties and interest can apply, and HMRC can issue discovery assessments going back multiple years.

## The Key Strategy: Pension Contributions

Pension contributions reduce your **adjusted net income** (ANI), which is the figure that drives both the personal allowance taper and the HICBC. Every pound contributed (gross) reduces ANI by one pound.

### Option A: Salary Sacrifice (Recommended)

Salary sacrifice is the most tax-efficient route because it saves both income tax and National Insurance. When you sacrifice salary into a pension:

- Your gross pay is reduced, so PAYE tax is recalculated on the lower figure automatically.
- You save **40% income tax** on the contribution.
- You also save **2% employee NI** on amounts above the Upper Earnings Limit of £50,270 (the rate above UEL for 2024/25).
- Your employer saves their employer NI too — some employers pass part of this saving back to employees as enhanced pension contributions.

**Crucially:** salary sacrifice reduces your contractual salary, which directly reduces ANI. No Self Assessment claim is needed to get the relief — it is automatic through payroll.

Side-effects to be aware of:
- A lower stated salary can affect mortgage applications and death-in-service benefits.
- Confirm with your employer how salary sacrifice interacts with any salary-related benefits.

### Option B: SIPP Contributions (Personal Contributions)

If salary sacrifice is not available in sufficient amounts, you can make personal contributions to your SIPP. The mechanism is "relief at source":

- You pay your net contribution into the SIPP.
- The SIPP provider automatically reclaims basic rate (20%) relief from HMRC, grossing up your contribution by 25%.
- You claim the **additional 20% higher rate relief** via your Self Assessment return — this is entered in the pension contributions section.

Importantly for your situation, SIPP contributions also reduce ANI for the purposes of the personal allowance taper and HICBC — making them doubly valuable.

### The Annual Allowance

For 2024/25 the pension annual allowance is **£60,000** (combined employer and employee contributions). Your income of £105,000 is well below the tapered annual allowance threshold (which only kicks in when adjusted income exceeds £260,000), so the full £60,000 allowance is available to you.

If you have unused annual allowance from the three previous tax years and were a member of a registered pension scheme, you may be able to carry forward and contribute more than £60,000 in a single year — useful if you want to make a large one-off contribution.

### Scenario 2: Contribute £25,000 to bring ANI to £80,000

| Item | After £25,000 contribution |
|------|---------------------------|
| Adjusted net income (ANI) | £80,000 |
| Personal allowance | £12,570 (fully restored) |

Note: to fully eliminate the HICBC you need ANI **below** £80,000. A contribution taking ANI to £79,999 would eliminate the charge entirely. £25,001 in contributions would achieve this.

Combined benefit of £25,001 contribution via salary sacrifice:
- Income tax relief at 40%: ~£10,000
- Extra relief from restoring full personal allowance: £1,000 (as per Scenario 1)
- NI saving at 2% on amounts above UEL: ~£500
- HICBC eliminated: £2,213
- **Total saving: approximately £13,713**
- **Real cost of £25,001 contribution: approximately £11,288**

### Scenario 3: Contribute £45,000 to bring ANI to £60,000

Taking ANI all the way to £60,000 eliminates the HICBC completely and puts you in the higher rate band throughout (no personal allowance taper). This is a major contribution but is within the £60,000 annual allowance.

## Your Wife and Marriage Allowance

Because your wife has no income, she is a non-taxpayer. Marriage Allowance allows a non-taxpaying spouse to transfer £1,260 of their personal allowance to a basic rate taxpaying spouse, saving up to £252 per year.

**However, this does not apply to you.** You are a higher rate taxpayer — Marriage Allowance is only available where the recipient is a basic rate taxpayer. You are ineligible. If you use pension contributions to reduce your taxable income below £50,270, you would technically become a basic rate taxpayer, but this is a very large reduction from £105,000 and the Marriage Allowance saving of £252 is small compared to the pension-driven savings available.

Your wife's unused personal allowance of £12,570 cannot otherwise be transferred to you.

## Child Benefit: Opt Out or Keep Claiming?

This is an important question for your wife specifically. The HICBC skill highlights a critical point:

**Do not simply opt out of Child Benefit without thinking about NI credits.**

Your wife is not working. A parent who is not working and caring for a child under 12 **receives Class 3 National Insurance credits** through the Child Benefit claim. These credits count toward the state pension entitlement (35 qualifying years needed for the full new state pension).

If she stops claiming Child Benefit, she stops accumulating NI credits, which could reduce her eventual state pension.

**The correct approach:** Use the HMRC "do not pay" option when registering for Child Benefit — this preserves the NI credit entitlement without actually receiving the payment (and therefore without triggering the HICBC). Alternatively, reduce ANI below £60,000 via pension contributions, which eliminates the charge and allows you to keep receiving the payments.

If you are already receiving Child Benefit and wish to keep it, the pension contribution strategy described above is the cleanest solution.

## Action Plan: Priority Order

1. **Increase salary sacrifice pension contributions immediately.** Calculate how much you need to sacrifice to bring your ANI to your target level (at minimum £100,000 to escape the 60% trap; ideally below £80,000 or £60,000 to reduce or eliminate the HICBC). Speak to your employer's HR/payroll team.

2. **Make additional SIPP contributions** if your employer's salary sacrifice scheme does not allow you to sacrifice enough to hit your target. Remember to claim the extra 20% higher rate relief on your Self Assessment return.

3. **Register for Self Assessment** if you have not already done so. You need to file because of the HICBC and because your income exceeds £100,000. The deadline to register for Self Assessment for the 2024/25 tax year is 5 October 2025.

4. **Check Child Benefit arrangements.** Ensure your wife's Child Benefit claim is structured to preserve her NI credits. If you plan to eliminate the HICBC via pension contributions, no change is needed. If not, switch to the "do not pay" option.

5. **Review the annual allowance.** Confirm your total employer and employee pension contributions for the year do not exceed £60,000. Ask your employer what they contribute — employer contributions count toward the annual allowance too.

6. **Consider carry forward** if you want to make a larger one-off SIPP contribution. You can use unused annual allowance from 2021/22, 2022/23, and 2023/24, provided you were a member of a registered pension scheme in those years.

7. **Gather your documents.** For your Self Assessment return you will need: P60, any P11D for benefits in kind, payslips showing pension contributions, and details of any personal SIPP contributions.
