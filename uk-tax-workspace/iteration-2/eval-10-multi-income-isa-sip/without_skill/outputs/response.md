## 2. Employment Income: £67,000

Your salary is taxed through PAYE. For 2024/25 the personal allowance is £12,570, the basic rate band runs to £50,270, and the higher rate of 40% applies on income between £50,270 and £125,140.

At £67,000 you are a higher rate taxpayer. This is relevant for every other part of your return because your dividend tax rate, savings rate, and CGT rate are all affected by your position in the tax bands.

You do not need to enter your salary in self assessment if HMRC already has it from your employer — they will pre-populate it. However, you should check it is correct on your return.

### Personal Allowance Taper

Your income is well below £100,000 so your personal allowance is the full £12,570 and is not being tapered. No action needed here.

## 3. Dividends: £3,400

For 2024/25 the dividend allowance is £500 (reduced from £1,000 in 2023/24 and from £2,000 before that).

- Dividends above £500 are taxable.
- Your taxable dividend income: £3,400 - £500 = £2,900.
- As a higher rate taxpayer, dividends in the higher rate band are taxed at 33.75%.
- Tax due on dividends: £2,900 x 33.75% = approximately £978.

Enter the full £3,400 in the "Dividends from UK companies" or "Dividends from foreign companies" box on your return (SA100, supplementary pages SA101 or via the online wizard). HMRC will apply the allowance automatically.

Note: dividends held inside your ISA are completely tax-free and do not go on the return.

## 4. Savings Interest: £950

For 2024/25, the Personal Savings Allowance (PSA) for a higher rate taxpayer is £500 (basic rate taxpayers get £1,000; additional rate taxpayers get £0).

- Interest above £500 is taxable.
- Your taxable interest: £950 - £500 = £450.
- Higher rate taxpayers pay 40% on savings interest above the PSA.
- Tax due on savings interest: £450 x 40% = £180.

Enter your total interest of £950 in the "Untaxed UK interest" box (box 1 or box 2 depending on whether tax was withheld). Most UK banks and building societies pay interest gross now, so it is likely untaxed.

## 5. Share Incentive Plan (SIP) — Free Shares: £3,600

SIP free shares are an HMRC-approved arrangement. The tax treatment depends entirely on how long you hold them:

- **Held less than 3 years**: Income tax and NI are due on their value when withdrawn.
- **Held between 3 and 5 years**: Income tax is due on the lower of the original market value or the value at withdrawal.
- **Held 5 or more years**: Completely free of income tax and NI.

You say you have not touched the shares. If they remain in the plan and you hold them for 5 years from the award date, you will pay no income tax or NI on them at all. **You do not include them on your self assessment return yet** — they are not a taxable event until you withdraw them from the plan, unless your employer has already assessed them via PAYE (which some do if shares are withdrawn early).

Check your P60 and P11D. If the £3,600 is already included in your P60 salary figure, it has been taxed through PAYE and you just need to report it accurately. If it is shown on a P11D as a benefit in kind, enter it in the employment pages of your return.

In most SIP free share arrangements where shares are still in the plan, no self assessment entry is needed for the current year.

## 6. SIP Partnership Shares: £1,800

Partnership shares are shares you buy out of your pre-tax salary. The key tax benefit is:

- The £1,800 is deducted from your gross salary before income tax and NI are calculated. This means you automatically save tax at your marginal rate.
- As a higher rate taxpayer: 40% income tax + 2% NI = approximately 42% relief on the purchase.
- Effective cost to you: roughly £1,044 for £1,800 worth of shares.

This deduction should already be reflected in your P60 — your taxable salary should show £1,800 less than your gross salary as a result. You do not separately claim partnership share relief on your self assessment return; it is handled via payroll.

If the partnership shares are still in the plan after 5 years, they also become free of income tax on withdrawal.

## 7. Capital Gains on Old Shares: £2,800

For 2024/25 the Capital Gains Tax (CGT) annual exempt amount is £3,000.

Your gain is £2,800, which is **below the £3,000 annual exempt amount**. Therefore:

- No CGT is due on this gain.
- However, you still need to report it on your self assessment return if your total proceeds from disposals in the year exceeded £50,000, or if you want to use the loss later, or if HMRC specifically requests it.

Practically: if your total sale proceeds were under £50,000 and you have no losses to carry forward, you may not need to report this gain at all — but it is best practice to report it anyway in the Capital Gains summary section.

### Important: Pre-UK Residency and CGT

You mention this was from shares bought before you moved to the UK. For CGT purposes, when you become UK resident, the base cost of assets is generally "rebased" to their market value on the date you became UK resident (if you were not previously UK-domiciled and elected for the remittance basis — but this is very specific). Under normal circumstances, the gain is calculated from the original acquisition price, not from when you arrived in the UK.

Given you have been here 3 years and the gain is small (£2,800), this is unlikely to be a major issue, but if the shares were worth significantly more when you arrived than when you bought them, you may wish to take advice on whether you can rebase the cost. For most straightforward cases with modest gains, report the gain using the original acquisition cost and the sale proceeds.

## 12. Are You Being Tax Efficient?

### What you are doing well

**ISA usage** — Contributing £10,000 to a stocks and shares ISA is excellent. All growth and income inside the ISA is completely sheltered. You have another £10,000 of ISA allowance remaining for 2024/25.

**SIP participation** — Both the free shares and partnership shares are strong benefits. The pre-tax purchase of partnership shares saves you roughly 42% in tax and NI. Holding the shares for 5 years means no income tax on withdrawal.

### Where you could improve tax efficiency

**Move more into your ISA** — Your dividends and savings interest are currently taxable because they sit in a General Investment Account (GIA) and a regular savings account. If you transferred those investments and savings into an ISA or cash ISA, future income would be tax-free. You cannot put cash from a GIA directly into an ISA (you would need to sell, then contribute the cash), so be aware of any CGT on gains in the GIA on sale.

**Use your full ISA allowance** — You have £10,000 of allowance remaining. Consider using it.

**Pension contributions** — If you are not maximising pension contributions, this is one of the most powerful ways to reduce your tax bill. As a higher rate taxpayer, every £1,000 of gross pension contribution saves you £400 in income tax. It also brings your adjusted net income down, which matters if you approach the £100,000 threshold (where the personal allowance starts to be withdrawn at a very punishing effective 60% rate — not relevant at £67,000, but worth knowing for future planning).

**Salary sacrifice pension** — If your employer offers salary sacrifice, this saves NI as well as income tax, making it even more efficient than a personal pension.

**Consider a cash ISA for savings** — Your £950 in savings interest exceeds your £500 PSA as a higher rate taxpayer. Moving savings into a cash ISA would shelter all future interest from tax.

**Dividend allowance** — The dividend allowance has been cut to £500. If your GIA holdings grow, your dividend tax bill will grow with them. Over time, sheltering dividend-paying shares inside your ISA is more valuable.

**Bed and ISA strategy** — For your GIA shares, consider a "Bed and ISA" approach: sell shares in the GIA to crystallise gains (using your £3,000 annual CGT exempt amount each year), then repurchase inside the ISA. This gradually moves your portfolio into the tax shelter. You can do up to £3,000 of gains per year without any CGT.

## 14. A Note on Your Residency

You have been in the UK for 3 years. For most people who arrived as full-year residents from year one, you are now straightforwardly UK-resident and UK-domiciled for tax purposes, and all worldwide income and gains are taxable in the UK. However, if there is any complexity around your domicile status or if you arrived mid-year, this can affect how overseas income and gains are treated. The old "non-domiciled remittance basis" rules changed significantly in April 2025 (replacing domicile with a 4-year foreign income exemption for new arrivals), so if you have any overseas income not mentioned above, it is worth checking whether any transitional provisions apply to you.
