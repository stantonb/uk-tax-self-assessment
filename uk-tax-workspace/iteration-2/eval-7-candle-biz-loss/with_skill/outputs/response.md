## 3. What Counts as a Deductible Business Expense?

This is where "the money I put in" needs to be broken down carefully, because different types of spending are treated differently.

### The Core Rule

An expense is deductible only if it is incurred **wholly and exclusively** for the purposes of the trade. This does not require items to be used solely for business — but the motivation for the spend must be entirely commercial.

### Equipment (Capital Expenditure)

The candle-making equipment you bought — moulds, thermometers, pouring jugs, wax melters, etc. — is **capital expenditure**, meaning it has lasting value beyond the accounting period. You cannot simply deduct the full cost as an ordinary expense under accruals accounting.

However, you can claim **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA)**, which provides 100% tax relief on qualifying plant and machinery in the year of purchase. This means you get the same practical result — full deduction — just through a different mechanism called a capital allowance.

**Alternatively**, if you use **cash basis accounting** (which is available to sole traders with turnover under £150,000 and is by far the most common choice for small side businesses), most capital expenditure on plant and machinery is simply deducted when paid. Cars are the notable exception. Cash basis makes your equipment costs straightforwardly deductible.

The practical upshot for most candle businesses: your equipment cost is fully deductible in 2024/25.

### Raw Materials and Supplies

Wax, wicks, fragrance oils, dyes, containers, packaging — these are **direct costs of sale** and are fully deductible when paid (under cash basis) or when incurred (under accruals). These are the clearest-cut expenses.

Important: if you have unsold stock at year-end, under accruals accounting you can only deduct the cost of goods actually sold. Under cash basis, you deduct when you pay, regardless of when the goods are sold or used. For a small side business, cash basis is simpler and likely more advantageous.

### Website

Website setup costs — domain registration, hosting, design — are allowable business expenses. If you used a platform subscription (Shopify, Squarespace, etc.), that too is deductible. Keep receipts and invoices.

### Summary of Your £8,000

Without a breakdown it is not possible to give precise figures, but broadly:

| Item | Treatment |
|------|-----------|
| Equipment (moulds, tools, etc.) | Fully deductible — AIA or cash basis |
| Raw materials and supplies | Fully deductible as cost of sale |
| Website setup and hosting | Fully deductible as allowable expense |
| Packaging materials | Fully deductible |

**You did not "invest" the £8,000 into the business in a way that disqualifies it from deduction.** The fact that the money came from your savings rather than a business account is irrelevant — what matters is that you spent it on legitimate business costs.

## 4. Working From Home: Your Spare Bedroom

Because you run the business from your spare bedroom in your rented flat, you can claim a proportion of your housing costs as a business expense. There are two methods — choose one and apply it consistently for the whole tax year.

### Method 1: Flat Rate (Simplest)

HMRC's simplified flat rate is based on hours worked from home each month:

| Hours per month | Monthly flat rate |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| 25 to 50 hours  | £10               |
| 51 to 100 hours | £18               |
| 101+ hours      | £26               |

If you work on the candle business most evenings and weekends, you are likely putting in well over 50 hours a month, giving you **£18/month or £216/year** for the months from June 2024 to April 2025 (10 months = £180).

The flat rate requires no receipts for household bills. It is simple, clean, and carries no risk of complications. The downside is that it may be much less than your actual proportion of costs.

### Method 2: Actual Costs (More Work, Potentially Higher Claim)

Under this method you calculate a business proportion of actual household costs:

**Step 1 — Room proportion**: If your flat has, say, 4 usable rooms (spare bedroom, living room, bedroom, kitchen excluded = 3 usable) and 1 is used for business, that is 1/3 = 33%.

**Step 2 — Time proportion**: The spare bedroom is not used exclusively for business — you likely use it as a guest room or for storage too. If you use it for business 40 hours per week out of 168 total hours in a week, that is approximately 24% business use.

**Combined proportion**: 33% x 24% = ~8% of household costs.

**Claimable costs include**:
- Your monthly rent (business proportion)
- Council tax (business proportion)
- Gas and electricity
- Water rates
- Broadband
- Home insurance (contents)

Example: if your annual rent is £14,400 (£1,200/month), claiming 8% gives you £1,152 for the full year, or approximately £960 for the 10 months of trading. This is substantially more than the flat rate.

### Important Note for Renters

Check your tenancy agreement. Many residential leases restrict or prohibit business use of the property. Before claiming — and more importantly before continuing to run the business from the flat — consider getting written permission from your landlord. This is a legal issue as much as a tax one.

There is **no Capital Gains Tax risk** for renters (unlike homeowners), so the "exclusive use" concern does not apply to you.

## 6. What Can You Do With the Loss?

This is the part your partner is thinking of. There are several options — but there is a crucial catch that many people miss.

### Option A: Carry Forward Against Future Profits

Your loss can be carried forward indefinitely and set against future profits from the **same candle business**. No time limit, no special claim needed — just declare the loss on your Self Assessment return and it rolls forward automatically.

This is the safest, most flexible option. But it only saves you tax once the business is profitable, which could be years away.

### Option B: Sideways Relief — Offset Against Your Salary

This is what your partner is probably imagining: using the business loss to reduce your employment income in the **same tax year**, thereby getting a tax refund.

Example: if your trading loss is £6,600 and your salary is £42,000:

- Taxable income without relief: £42,000 - £12,570 (personal allowance) = £29,430 taxed at 20% = £5,886 tax
- Taxable income with sideways relief: (£42,000 - £6,600) = £35,400 - £12,570 = £22,830 taxed at 20% = £4,566 tax
- **Tax saving: approximately £1,320** (20% of £6,600)

This is a real, cashable refund — HMRC would effectively repay some of the income tax you have already paid through PAYE.

### THE CRITICAL CATCH: Cash Basis Blocks Sideways Relief

Here is the issue that trips up many new sole traders:

**If you use cash basis accounting, you cannot claim sideways relief or carry back. You can only carry the loss forward.**

Cash basis is the default for most new small businesses, and it is almost certainly what you would naturally use. But its rules explicitly exclude sideways relief and carry back for trading losses.

**To claim sideways relief against your salary, you must use accruals basis accounting instead.**

Accruals basis is slightly more complex:
- Income is recognised when earned (invoiced), not when received
- Expenses are recognised when incurred, not when paid
- You must value any unsold stock at year-end

For a simple candle business at this scale, switching to accruals is not enormously complicated, but it is a deliberate choice you must make before completing your tax return — you cannot switch retrospectively once the return is filed.

**This is one of the most important decisions you need to make now.** If a tax refund of ~£1,000+ is meaningful to you, accruals basis is worth considering. Get advice from an accountant to confirm whether it makes sense for your specific figures.

### Option C: Early Years Carry Back

Because you are in the **first four years of a new trade**, you have access to Early Years Relief. This lets you carry the loss back against your general income (including salary) of the **three tax years before the loss year**, taking the earliest year first.

For the 2024/25 loss, you could carry it back to 2021/22, then 2022/23, then 2023/24.

This is valuable if your income was similar or higher in prior years — it could generate a refund of tax already paid in those years. Again, this requires accruals basis — the cash basis restriction applies equally here.

## 7. Is Your Business a Genuine Trade or a Hobby?

This is important because HMRC will not allow sideways relief if they conclude your activity is a **hobby** rather than a genuine commercial trade.

HMRC considers:
- Is the activity conducted with a genuine view to making a profit?
- Is it run in a businesslike manner?
- How much time do you devote to it?
- Is it similar to a recognised trade?
- Have profits been made, or is there a realistic expectation of profit?

A candle-making business started in June 2024 with a website, proper supplies, and real sales of £1,400 in its early months is a reasonable case for a genuine trade. The facts support this:
- You invested money in equipment and a website (businesslike setup)
- You have made actual sales
- You are operating evenings and weekends (real time commitment)
- You are asking about tax compliance (businesslike attitude)

However, HMRC scrutinises losses in craft/hobby-adjacent businesses more closely. **Do keep evidence of your commercial intent**: records of pricing decisions, marketing activity, business planning, and any steps taken to grow sales. If the business continues to make losses year after year without a plan for profitability, HMRC could challenge future sideways relief claims.

The good news: your loss in year one is perfectly explicable — you had £8,000 of startup costs in a year you only traded for 10 months. That is entirely normal for a new business.

## 9. National Insurance

Since your candle business has made a loss (no profit), there is **no Class 4 NI liability**. Class 4 NI is charged on self-employment profits, and a loss means zero profits.

Regarding **Class 2 NI**: from April 2024, Class 2 NI is no longer mandatory if profits are below the Small Profits Threshold (£6,725). With a loss, you are well below this. You can make voluntary Class 2 contributions at £3.45/week to protect your state pension entitlement — worth considering if this year is otherwise a "gap" year.

## Summary

Yes, your partner is right in principle. The £8,000 you spent on equipment, supplies, and the website is — subject to correct categorisation — deductible business expenditure. Combined with your £1,400 of revenue, this almost certainly produces a trading loss.

The key decision is **what to do with that loss**:
- Carry it forward (simple, no tax saving now)
- Claim sideways relief against your £42,000 salary (potential refund of ~20% of the loss — but only possible if you use accruals basis, not cash basis)
- Carry it back under Early Years Relief (could reclaim tax from 2021/22 to 2023/24)

The **cash basis restriction on sideways relief** is the most important thing to understand. Most guides do not highlight this clearly enough. If getting a tax refund now matters to you, you need to actively opt for accruals basis accounting before filing your return.

Keep all your records, register without delay, and consider getting an accountant to help with this first return given the moving parts involved.
