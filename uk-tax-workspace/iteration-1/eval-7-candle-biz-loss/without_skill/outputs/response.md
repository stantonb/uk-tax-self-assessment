# UK Tax Self-Assessment: Candle-Making Business Loss

## Your Situation in Brief

You have a salaried job (£42,000/year) and started a sole trader side business in June 2024. You invested £8,000 and earned £1,400 in sales, giving you an approximate trading loss. Here is how this works for UK tax purposes.

---

## 1. Does the Money You Put In Count as a Business Expense?

This depends on **what the £8,000 was spent on**. It is not one single category — you need to break it down:

### a) Revenue (Day-to-Day) Expenses
Things like wax, wicks, fragrance oils, packaging, and website hosting fees are **revenue expenses**. These are fully deductible from your business income in the year they are incurred. They reduce your profit (or increase your loss) directly.

### b) Capital Equipment
If you bought equipment (e.g., a melting pot, scales, a moulds set, a camera for product photos), these are **capital assets**. Under normal rules you would depreciate them, but HMRC offers a relief called the **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA)**, which lets most small businesses deduct the full cost of qualifying equipment in the year of purchase rather than over several years. For a sole trader at your scale, this almost certainly means you can claim the full equipment cost upfront as a capital allowance.

### c) The Website
Initial website **setup costs** may be treated as capital (with AIA available), while ongoing hosting/domain fees are revenue expenses.

### d) Your Own Savings as "Investment"
The fact that the money came from your own savings does not matter. What matters is **what you spent it on**. You do not get a deduction simply for "putting money into the business" — the deduction comes from the individual items of expenditure.

**Practical step:** List out every item of the £8,000 spend and categorise it as supplies/consumables (revenue) vs. equipment (capital). Both categories should give you a full deduction in the 2024/25 tax year.

---

## 2. Working Out Your Business Loss

A rough calculation for the year ending 5 April 2025 (covering June 2024 to April 2025):

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Sales income | £1,400 |
| Less: allowable expenses (supplies, packaging, website costs, etc.) | (e.g., £3,000) |
| Less: capital allowances (equipment) | (e.g., £4,000) |
| **Trading loss** | **(approx. £5,600)** |

The exact figures depend on your actual spend breakdown. You may also be able to claim a proportion of your home broadband and phone costs if used for the business, and a **use of home as office** allowance (HMRC's simplified flat rate is £6/week, or you can calculate actual costs based on the proportion of your flat used and hours worked).

---

## 3. Can You Get Tax Back on the Loss? — Sideways Loss Relief

Yes — this is what your partner is likely referring to, and it is a genuine and legitimate relief. It is called **sideways loss relief** (under Section 64 of the Income Tax Act 2007).

### How It Works

If your candle business makes a trading loss, you can **offset that loss against your other income in the same tax year** — including your £42,000 salary. This reduces your total taxable income, meaning you pay less income tax (and potentially NI) overall, and if you have already paid too much through PAYE, you receive a refund.

### Example

Assuming a trading loss of £6,600 (i.e., £8,000 costs minus £1,400 income):

- Salary: £42,000
- Less trading loss relief: (£6,600)
- Adjusted taxable income: £35,400

At a basic rate of 20% tax, that loss relief saves you approximately **£1,320 in income tax** (the portion of your salary that falls in the basic rate band). If some of the loss offsets salary above £50,270, relief would be at 40%.

You would claim this via **Self Assessment**. You will need to register as self-employed and file a tax return for 2024/25 (deadline: 31 January 2026 for online filing).

---

## 4. Important Conditions and Caveats

### "Badges of Trade" / Genuine Commercial Intent
HMRC will only allow these reliefs if your business is run **on a commercial basis with a view to making a profit**. You must be able to show:
- You have a genuine intention to make the business profitable (not just a hobby).
- You keep records of income and expenses.
- You are actively trying to grow sales.

If HMRC decides it is a hobby rather than a trade, none of the business expenses or loss relief will be available.

### The "Hobby" vs. "Trade" Line
There is no bright-line rule, but factors that support "trade" include: a business plan, a dedicated trading name or website, marketing activity, professional invoicing, and improving turnover over time. Given you have a website and real sales, you are in a reasonable position — but keep evidence of your commercial intent.

### Sideways Loss Relief Limit
Sideways loss relief is capped at the **greater of £50,000 or 25% of your adjusted total income** in any one year. This is very unlikely to be an issue at your scale.

### Timing: Carry Back Option
Alternatively (or in addition), you can carry a trading loss **back one year** against income from the previous tax year (2023/24), or carry it **forward** against future profits from the same trade. Carrying it sideways (against your salary in the same year) is usually the most tax-efficient option if you have already paid income tax via PAYE.

---

## 5. Practical Steps

1. **Register for Self Assessment** with HMRC if you have not already done so (you should register by 5 October 2025 for the 2024/25 tax year).
2. **Keep all receipts and records** for every item of expenditure.
3. **Complete a Self Assessment tax return** (SA100) including the self-employment pages (SA103).
4. On the return, claim your allowable expenses, capital allowances, and elect to set the loss against other income (sideways loss relief).
5. Consider consulting a qualified accountant or tax adviser — the cost of one session is itself a deductible business expense, and they can ensure you maximise your legitimate reliefs without errors.

---

## Summary

Yes, the money you spent can count as business expenses and capital allowances, reducing your taxable profit to a loss. Yes, you can claim that loss against your salary and receive a tax refund. The key requirements are that you are running a genuine commercial business (not a hobby), you keep proper records, and you file a Self Assessment return. The relief is real and commonly used by people in exactly your position.
