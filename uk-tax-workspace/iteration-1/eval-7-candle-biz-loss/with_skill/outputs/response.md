# Your Candle Business and Tax: What You Need to Know

Your situation is a common one for people starting a business alongside employment. There is genuine tax relief available to you, but the mechanics are specific and worth understanding properly before you act. Here is a full picture.

---

## First: What Is Your Trading Loss?

You have spent £8,000 (equipment, supplies, website) and earned £1,400 in sales. However, the tax calculation is not simply £8,000 minus £1,400. The question is which parts of the £8,000 are **allowable business expenses**, and how they are treated for tax.

### Revenue Expenses (deduct in full this year)

These are day-to-day costs consumed in running the business:

- **Candle-making supplies** (wax, wicks, fragrance, jars, packaging) — fully deductible as stock/materials
- **Website setup and hosting costs** — fully deductible as marketing/IT costs
- **Any consumable tools** (items that do not last long-term)

### Capital Expenditure (equipment with lasting value)

If any of the £8,000 went on equipment that will last and be used in future years (e.g., a wax melter, pour pots, moulds, a label printer), this is **capital expenditure**, not a revenue expense. You cannot simply deduct it as a day-to-day cost.

However, you can claim it through **capital allowances**, specifically the **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA)**, which gives you 100% relief in the year of purchase. For a side business of this size, the AIA limit (currently very generous at £1 million) will not be an issue. The practical effect is the same — full deduction in year one — but it must be declared in the correct section of the Self Assessment return.

**If you are using the cash basis** (available since your turnover is well below £150,000), capital expenditure on most plant and machinery is simply deducted when paid. Cars are excluded, but candle-making equipment is not. The cash basis simplifies this considerably for you.

### The Core Rule

Every expense must be **wholly and exclusively** for the business. The candle supplies, website, and equipment you described sound clearly business-related. If anything has personal use as well (for example, a laptop you also use at home for personal browsing), you can only claim the business proportion.

---

## What Is the Trading Loss?

Assuming all £8,000 is allowable (either as revenue expenses or via AIA/cash basis capital deductions), and your only income is £1,400 in sales:

**Trading loss = £1,400 income minus £8,000 allowable costs = -£6,600 loss**

This loss is real for tax purposes and can be used to reduce your tax bill.

---

## Can You "Write It Off" Against Your Salary? (Sideways Loss Relief)

Yes — this is what your partner is referring to. It is called **sideways loss relief**, and it works as follows:

You can offset the trading loss from your candle business against your employment income in the **same tax year**. Since you started in June 2024, your loss falls in the **2024/25 tax year** (6 April 2024 to 5 April 2025).

### Your Numbers

| | |
|---|---|
| Salary (employment income) | £42,000 |
| Less: trading loss (sideways relief) | -£6,600 |
| **Adjusted taxable income** | **£35,400** |

Your personal allowance is £12,570. Without the loss relief, your taxable income is £42,000 - £12,570 = **£29,430**, all taxed at 20%.

With the loss relief applied, your taxable income is £35,400 - £12,570 = **£22,830**, all still within the basic rate band.

**Tax saving: £6,600 x 20% = £1,320**

In practical terms, you would receive a repayment or reduction in your tax bill of approximately **£1,320**.

---

## Early Years Relief — An Additional Option

Because this is the **first tax year of your trade** (you started in June 2024), you have access to a more powerful form of relief: **Early Years Loss Relief** (also called "backwards carry-back for new trades").

This allows you to carry the loss back against your general income in the **three tax years before the loss year** — so potentially 2023/24, 2022/23, and 2021/22 — starting with the earliest year first.

This could be valuable if:
- You were in a **higher tax bracket** in a prior year
- You want to receive a **cash repayment now** rather than waiting for a future year

If your income was similar in prior years, the saving would still be 20%, same as the current year. But if you had a year where income was higher, it could be worth exploring.

## Important Restrictions — Will HMRC Allow This?

Sideways loss relief is not automatic. HMRC applies scrutiny, especially for new or small side businesses. The key risk to be aware of:

### Hobby vs Trade Test

HMRC can deny sideways loss relief if they conclude your business is a **hobby** rather than a genuine commercial trade. They assess this by asking:

- Is the activity conducted with a genuine expectation of making a profit?
- Is it run in a businesslike way (records kept, pricing reviewed, marketing done)?
- How much time is dedicated to it?
- Are you taking steps to grow revenue?

Your candle business sounds like it passes this test — you have invested meaningfully, set up a website, and are making sales. The fact that you have only made £1,400 so far is not in itself a problem; new businesses take time to become profitable. However, you should be able to demonstrate that you are **running it as a business**, not merely as a creative hobby.

**Keep records of**: your pricing decisions, sales activity, marketing efforts, and any business planning. This is your evidence that it is a genuine trade.

### Cash Basis Restriction

If you use the **cash basis** of accounting (which is likely the default for a small new business), there is an important limitation: **sideways loss relief and carry-back are generally not available under cash basis**. You can only carry the loss forward against future profits of the same trade.

**This is a critical point.** If you want to claim sideways relief against your salary this year, you may need to **opt out of the cash basis** and use the **accruals basis** instead. A tax adviser or accountant can help you assess whether this is worthwhile given the amounts involved. Given the £1,320 potential saving, it is likely worth considering.

---

## What If You Cannot or Do Not Claim Sideways Relief?

If you stay on the cash basis, or choose not to claim sideways relief, you can **carry the loss forward** instead. This means:

- The £6,600 loss is recorded on your Self Assessment return
- In future years when the candle business makes a profit, the loss is deducted from that profit first
- You only pay tax on profits above the carried-forward loss

This is automatic — you just declare the loss and it carries forward indefinitely until used. No tax saving now, but it reduces your future tax bill.

## The £1,000 Trading Allowance: Ignore It Here

HMRC offers a £1,000 "trading allowance" — if your gross income is below £1,000, you do not need to register or report. Your income is £1,400, so you are above this threshold and must register and file. More importantly, **claiming the trading allowance instead of actual expenses would cost you dearly** here — you would lose the ability to deduct your actual £8,000 costs and claim the loss.

## What About Working From Home?

You mention using your spare bedroom evenings and weekends. As a self-employed person (not as an employee), you can claim a deduction for home office costs. You have two options:

**Option 1: Flat rate (simplified expenses)**
Based on hours worked from home per month:
- 25–50 hours/month: £10/month
- 51–100 hours/month: £18/month
- 101+ hours/month: £26/month

If you work on it most evenings and weekends, you might reasonably be in the 51–100 hour bracket, giving you **£18 x 10 months (June to March) = £180** for the 2024/25 year. Simple, no receipts needed.

**Option 2: Actual cost proportion**
Calculate the proportion of your rent, utilities, broadband, and council tax attributable to the spare bedroom and the hours you use it for business. This is more work but could yield a higher deduction if your rent is significant.

Since you rent (you do not own), there is no Capital Gains Tax complication to worry about with the home office.

Note: you cannot claim both the employed WFH flat rate (if your employer pays it or you claim it for PAYE purposes) and the self-employed home office costs for the same space and time period.

## Registration and Filing — Action Points

Your gross income of £1,400 exceeds the £1,000 trading allowance, so you **must register** for Self Assessment and file a return.

**Deadline to register**: 5 October 2025 (for the 2024/25 tax year)
**Deadline to file online**: 31 January 2026
**Deadline to pay any tax**: 31 January 2026

Since you are making a loss (no tax to pay on the business), registering is still required — and in fact essential if you want to claim loss relief against your salary.

Register at gov.uk (search "register for Self Assessment"). HMRC will issue a Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR) by post, typically within 10 working days.

On your Self Assessment return:
- Report your employment income using figures from your P60
- Complete the self-employment pages (SA103) with your candle business income and expenses
- Claim the trading loss and elect for sideways relief if you want to offset it against your salary
- Sideways relief must be claimed within **12 months of the 31 January filing deadline** for the relevant tax year (so by 31 January 2027 for 2024/25)

## Key Watch-Outs

1. **Cash basis vs accruals**: If you want sideways relief this year, you may need to opt out of the cash basis. Get advice on this before filing.
2. **Substantiate that it is a genuine trade**: keep records that show you are running this commercially, not as a hobby.
3. **Register by 5 October 2025** if you have not already done so.
4. **Keep all receipts** for the full £8,000 spent — digital copies are fine. HMRC can ask to see records up to 5 years after the 31 January filing deadline.
5. **Consider an accountant** for this first return. The loss relief election and the cash basis decision involve judgment calls, and the cost of an accountant (itself deductible as a business expense in future years) may well be less than the tax saving at stake.
