## Do You Need to File a Self Assessment Return?

**Yes, almost certainly — and for two separate reasons.**

The key Self Assessment filing triggers that apply to you are:

1. **Dividends above the dividend allowance**: Your £2,300 in dividends exceeds the £500 dividend allowance for 2024/25. Any dividends above this allowance must be reported via Self Assessment.

2. **Savings interest above your Personal Savings Allowance**: As explained below, £800 of your savings interest falls outside any tax-free allowances and is taxable — this also triggers a Self Assessment obligation.

HMRC does sometimes collect small amounts of tax on savings interest by adjusting your tax code rather than requiring a return, but the dividend excess makes Self Assessment filing necessary regardless.

### Step 2 — Starting Rate for Savings

This is a valuable allowance that many retirees qualify for and is often overlooked.

- The starting rate for savings is 0% on up to **£5,000** of savings income.
- However, it is reduced by £1 for every £1 of non-savings income that exceeds the Personal Allowance.

Your non-savings income above the Personal Allowance:

| | |
|---|---|
| Pension income | £14,000 |
| Less: Personal Allowance | (£12,570) |
| Non-savings income above PA | £1,430 |

Starting rate band available = £5,000 − £1,430 = **£3,570**

Your savings interest of £1,800 is less than the £3,570 available starting rate band. This means **all £1,800 of your savings interest falls within the starting rate band at 0%**.

---

### Step 3 — Personal Savings Allowance (PSA)

As a basic rate taxpayer, you receive a Personal Savings Allowance of **£1,000**.

However, because all of your savings interest (£1,800) is already covered by the starting rate for savings, the PSA is not needed here — you have no remaining taxable savings interest regardless.

**Tax on savings interest: £0**

## What You Need to Do

1. **Register for Self Assessment** if you are not already registered — do this via HMRC's online service or by completing form SA1. You can register at: [https://www.gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment](https://www.gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment)

2. **Key deadlines for the 2024/25 tax year** (6 April 2024 to 5 April 2025):
   - **5 October 2025**: Deadline to register for Self Assessment if this is your first return
   - **31 October 2025**: Paper return deadline
   - **31 January 2026**: Online return deadline AND payment deadline

3. **Gather your records**:
   - P60 or annual statement from your pension provider (showing gross pension and tax deducted)
   - Interest certificates or annual summaries from all savings accounts
   - Dividend statements or broker tax certificates for your share portfolio
   - Keep all records for at least 5 years after the 31 January filing deadline

## Key Points to Note

- **Savings interest**: The starting rate for savings is particularly valuable in your situation. Because your pension is relatively modest, a large portion of your savings rate band is still available, meaning your £1,800 of interest is entirely tax-free.

- **Dividends in an ISA**: You mentioned your shares are not in an ISA. If you are able to transfer some holdings into a Stocks and Shares ISA (up to £20,000/year), future dividends from those shares would be completely tax-free and would not need to be reported. This could eliminate the dividend tax liability in future years.

- **HMRC automatic adjustment**: For savings interest alone, HMRC sometimes adjusts a pensioner's tax code automatically rather than requiring a full return. However, because you also have taxable dividends above the allowance, Self Assessment is the correct route.

- **Marriage Allowance**: If you have a spouse or civil partner with income below the Personal Allowance (£12,570), they may be able to transfer £1,260 of their unused Personal Allowance to you, saving up to £252 in tax. Mention this to a tax adviser if applicable.
