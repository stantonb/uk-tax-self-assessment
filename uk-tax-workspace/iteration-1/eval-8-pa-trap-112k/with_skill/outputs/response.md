# Your Tax Situation at £112,000 — What's Happening and What You Can Do

Your accountant friend is absolutely right, and this is genuinely one of the most important tax planning situations in the UK system. Let me break down exactly what's happening, then work through your numbers specifically.

---

## Part 1: Why You're Facing an Effective 60% Rate

### The Personal Allowance Trap Explained

At £112,000, you have fallen into what is commonly called the "£100k trap" or "personal allowance trap."

Here is the mechanism:

- Your personal allowance is normally £12,570 — the first £12,570 of your income is tax-free.
- For every £2 your adjusted net income (ANI) exceeds £100,000, you lose £1 of that personal allowance.
- At your current income of £112,000, you are £12,000 over the £100,000 threshold.
- £12,000 ÷ 2 = £6,000 of personal allowance lost.
- So you only have £12,570 − £6,000 = **£6,570 of personal allowance remaining**.

### Why This Creates a ~60% Effective Rate

On the income between £100,000 and £125,140, you simultaneously:

1. Pay **40% higher rate income tax** on that income itself, AND
2. Lose **£1 of tax-free allowance** for every £2 earned — which means £1 of previously untaxed income becomes taxable at 40%.

The combined effect: 40% tax on the income itself + 40% tax on the lost allowance = effectively **60% marginal rate** on income in this band.

This is entirely legal and deliberate policy — it is not a mistake. But it is avoidable with the right planning.

## Part 3: The High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC)

This is a second hit you are facing that compounds the problem significantly.

### How Child Benefit Works at Your Income

You have 3 children. The weekly Child Benefit you receive is:
- Eldest child: £25.60/week
- Child 2: £16.95/week
- Child 3: £16.95/week
- **Total weekly: £59.50**
- **Total annual Child Benefit: £59.50 × 52 = £3,094**

The HICBC charges 1% of the total Child Benefit for every £200 of ANI above £60,000. The charge reaches 100% (full clawback) at £80,000.

At your current ANI of **£106,400**, you are above £80,000, which means:

**You are currently repaying 100% of your Child Benefit — the full £3,094 — via the HICBC.**

You are receiving Child Benefit with one hand and handing all of it back via your tax return with the other. At your income level, the HICBC is a total clawback.

### Important: You Must File Self Assessment

Because you have a HICBC liability, you are legally required to file a Self Assessment return — even though you are employed and pay tax via PAYE. If you have not already registered for Self Assessment, you must do so. HMRC can pursue unpaid HICBC going back multiple years, with penalties and interest.

Key 2024/25 deadlines:
- **5 October 2025**: Register for Self Assessment if this is your first return
- **31 January 2026**: Online return and payment deadline
- **31 July 2026**: Second payment on account

## Part 4: The Solution — Increase Your Pension Contributions

The single most powerful thing you can do is **increase your salary sacrifice pension contributions to bring your ANI down to £100,000**. This achieves multiple goals simultaneously.

### The Target

You need ANI ≤ £100,000 to:
1. Recover your full £12,570 personal allowance
2. Eliminate the 60% effective marginal rate zone entirely
3. Eliminate the HICBC charge completely (you are already above the £80,000 full clawback threshold, so any reduction toward £100,000 saves nothing on HICBC — you need to get to £80,000 to start saving HICBC, and to £60,000 to eliminate it entirely)

Let's work through the pension maths carefully.

### Scenario A: Get ANI to £100,000 (Eliminate the PA Trap)

Current ANI: £106,400
Target ANI: £100,000
Additional pension contribution needed: **£6,400**

If you increase your salary sacrifice by £6,400 per year (approximately £533/month extra):

- Your ANI drops to £100,000
- Personal allowance fully restored to £12,570
- The 60% effective rate zone is eliminated
- You gain back tax on £6,570 of income at 40% = **£2,628 in tax savings**
- You also save employee NI at 2% on £6,400 (you are above the Upper Earnings Limit, so only 2% NI applies): £6,400 × 2% = **£128 in NI savings**
- Total tax and NI saved: approximately **£2,756**
- Net cost of the additional £6,400 into your pension: £6,400 − £2,756 = approximately **£3,644**

For every £1 you put into your pension in this range, it costs you approximately **57p** after tax and NI relief. The other 43p is effectively paid by the government via tax relief.

But this only gets you out of the PA trap. You are still well above the HICBC full clawback threshold, so you would still lose all your Child Benefit.

### Scenario B: Get ANI to £80,000 (Start Recovering Child Benefit)

Current ANI: £106,400
Target ANI: £80,000
Additional pension contribution needed: **£26,400**

At ANI of £80,000, the HICBC charge drops to 100% (you hit the upper limit at £80,000 exactly). To start recovering Child Benefit, you need ANI below £80,000.

### Scenario C: Get ANI to £60,000 (Eliminate HICBC Entirely)

Current ANI: £106,400
Target ANI: £60,000
Additional pension contribution needed: **£46,400**

If you can get your ANI down to £60,000:
- Full personal allowance restored: saves ~£5,028 in income tax (recovering full £12,570 PA at 40%)
- HICBC eliminated: saves **£3,094/year**
- Total annual saving: approximately **£8,122 in tax** (plus NI savings on the contributions)

However, getting ANI from £106,400 to £60,000 requires an additional £46,400 in pension contributions on top of your existing £5,600. That is a large commitment. Let's check the annual allowance.

### Annual Allowance Check

The annual allowance is **£60,000** for 2024/25. Both employer and employee contributions count:

- Employer 5%: £5,600
- Your current 5%: £5,600
- Total currently going in: **£11,200**
- Remaining annual allowance: £60,000 − £11,200 = **£48,800**

An additional £46,400 would bring total contributions to £57,600 — within the £60,000 annual allowance. So the annual allowance does not block this.

Also note: The tapered annual allowance does not apply to you. It only kicks in at adjusted income above £260,000. You are nowhere near that.

If you want to make an even larger one-off contribution to clear back-years of HICBC, you may be able to use **carry forward** — unused annual allowance from the previous 3 tax years. This is worth exploring if you have not been making significant pension contributions previously.

## Part 7: Recommended Action Plan

Here is a prioritised plan:

### Step 1 — Increase Salary Sacrifice Immediately (High Priority)

Talk to your HR or payroll department about increasing your salary sacrifice pension contribution. Even if you cannot get all the way to £60,000 ANI, every pound you sacrifice above £100,000 ANI is taxed at an effective 60% relief rate — it is the most tax-efficient pension contribution you will ever make.

**Minimum target**: Get ANI to £100,000 (additional £6,400 per year) to escape the PA trap.
**Better target**: Get ANI to £80,000 (additional £26,400 per year) to begin recovering child benefit.
**Optimal target**: Get ANI to £60,000 (additional £46,400 per year) to eliminate HICBC entirely.

### Step 2 — Register for Self Assessment Now (Urgent if not already done)

You have a HICBC liability and your income exceeds £100,000 — both are triggers for Self Assessment. Register at gov.uk/log-in-file-self-assessment-tax-return. The registration deadline for 2024/25 is 5 October 2025; the filing and payment deadline is 31 January 2026.

### Step 3 — Declare Child Benefit on Your Return

On your Self Assessment return, declare the total Child Benefit your household received in the tax year. HMRC will calculate the HICBC charge. If you have already increased pension contributions to bring ANI below £80,000, the charge will reduce proportionally; below £60,000, it disappears entirely.

### Step 4 — Consider Carry Forward for a Larger One-Off Contribution

If you have been in your pension scheme for the last 3 years and have not used the full annual allowance, you may be able to make a larger one-off contribution this year, using carry forward. This could allow you to make a substantial lump sum payment to bring ANI into a better position. Discuss this with your pension provider and a financial adviser.

### Step 5 — Review Your Tax Code

HMRC should be adjusting your PAYE tax code to collect the HICBC and to reflect any reduction in personal allowance. Check your tax code notice (P2) — if it has not been updated, you may be under-paying tax through the year, which creates a larger bill in January. If you increase pension contributions significantly, contact HMRC to update your code.
