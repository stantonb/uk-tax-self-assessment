# UK Tax Planning at £112,000: The Personal Allowance Trap

## The 60% Tax Trap — Your Accountant Is Right

Your accountant friend is absolutely correct, and it is one of the most brutal quirks in the UK tax system.

Here is what happens:

- Everyone gets a Personal Allowance of **£12,570** (2025/26) — income you pay no tax on.
- Once your income exceeds **£100,000**, HMRC claws back your Personal Allowance at a rate of **£1 for every £2** you earn above £100,000.
- This means your Personal Allowance is completely gone by the time your income reaches **£125,140**.

At £112,000, you are squarely in this trap. On the £12,000 you earn between £100,000 and £112,000:

- You pay 40% Income Tax on it (higher rate band)
- AND you lose £6,000 of your Personal Allowance (£12,000 / 2)
- That £6,000 of previously tax-free income now gets taxed at 40%

**Effective rate on that slice = 40% + 40% = 60%**

So yes — for every £2 you earn between £100,000 and £125,140, you effectively pay £1.20 in income tax. That is a 60% marginal rate. It is not a myth, it is real, and it is completely legal (just deeply unfair-feeling).

## Your Current Salary Sacrifice Pension

You earn £112,000. Your employer puts in 5% and you put in 5% via **salary sacrifice**.

Salary sacrifice is key here: it reduces your **contractual salary** for tax and NI purposes. So if your employer calculates 5% of £112,000 = £5,600 employee contribution via salary sacrifice, your taxable income drops to approximately:

- £112,000 - £5,600 (your salary sacrifice) = **£106,400** taxable income

Your employer's 5% (£5,600) goes on top but does not count as your income. So your adjusted net income for tax purposes is around **£106,400** — you are still well inside the Personal Allowance trap (£100,000–£125,140).

## Child Benefit: The High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC)

This is a second trap you need to know about.

Child Benefit for 3 children (2025/26 rates):
- Eldest child: **£25.60/week** = ~£1,331/year
- Each additional child: **£16.95/week** = ~£881/year each
- Total for 3 children: ~**£3,094/year**

The HICBC claws back Child Benefit when the **higher earner** in the household has income over **£60,000**:
- You repay 1% of Child Benefit for every £200 of income above £60,000
- At £80,000+, you repay 100% — you effectively get nothing

At your income level (£106,400 after salary sacrifice), you are **well above £80,000**, so you are currently repaying **all** of your Child Benefit through your Self Assessment tax return. You are getting ~£3,094/year but paying it all back. Net benefit: **£0**.

**Important:** The HICBC is based on "adjusted net income" — which is your income *after* pension contributions (salary sacrifice reduces this). So pension contributions can help here too.

## What You Should Do: Increase Your Pension Contributions

This is the single most powerful lever available to you.

### The Maths

Your adjusted net income (after current salary sacrifice) is approximately **£106,400**.

To escape the Personal Allowance trap entirely, you need to get your adjusted net income below **£100,000**.

That means contributing an additional **£6,400** to your pension via salary sacrifice.

### What You Get Back

By reducing your income from ~£106,400 to £100,000 via extra pension contributions of ~£6,400:

1. **60% effective tax relief** on those contributions (because you're in the 60% marginal zone)
   - Every £100 extra into your pension only costs you £40 net

2. **You restore your full Personal Allowance** (worth £5,028 in tax at 40% = £5,028 saved)

3. **If you can get below £80,000** — which requires significantly larger contributions — you start getting to keep some Child Benefit. To get below £80,000, you would need ~£26,400 in additional pension contributions, which may or may not be practical.

### Annual Allowance Check

The pension Annual Allowance for 2025/26 is **£60,000** (including employer contributions). You currently contribute:
- Employer: 5% of £112,000 = £5,600
- You: 5% of £112,000 = £5,600
- Total: **£11,200**

You have plenty of headroom — up to ~£48,800 more before hitting the Annual Allowance. (You can also carry forward unused allowances from the previous 3 tax years if needed.)

## Concrete Recommendation

**Step 1: Increase salary sacrifice to bring adjusted net income to £100,000.**

Work out your exact figures with payroll, but roughly: increase your personal pension contribution from 5% to around **10-11%** via salary sacrifice. This should get your adjusted net income to ~£100,000.

At that point:
- You restore your full £12,570 Personal Allowance
- You get 60% effective tax relief on those extra contributions
- Every £1 extra in your pension costs you only ~40p net

**Step 2: Consider going further to recover Child Benefit.**

To recover Child Benefit meaningfully, you need adjusted net income below £80,000 — that is an additional ~£20,000+ in pension contributions on top of Step 1. Whether that is feasible depends on your cash flow. If you can do it, you recover ~£3,094/year in Child Benefit AND get 40% tax relief on those contributions.

**Step 3: File a Self Assessment tax return.**

At £100,000+ income, HMRC requires you to file Self Assessment. Make sure you are registered. This is also where you declare HICBC if applicable and claim pension relief if any contributions are made outside salary sacrifice.

## Important Caveats

- These figures are based on **2025/26 tax year** rates and thresholds.
- "Adjusted net income" has a specific HMRC definition — it includes things like Gift Aid donations as well as pension contributions, which can also help reduce it.
- Salary sacrifice must be agreed with your employer — most workplace schemes allow this but check yours.
- I am not a financial adviser. For your specific situation, especially given the amounts involved, it is worth getting proper regulated financial advice. The maths here strongly favours increased pension contributions, but a financial adviser can look at your full picture (mortgage, ISAs, retirement timeline, etc.).

The core message: **yes, increase your pension contributions via salary sacrifice, prioritise getting your adjusted net income to at least £100,000, and ideally lower**. The tax relief at this income level is extraordinary — it is one of the rare cases where putting money into a pension is a near-automatic decision.
