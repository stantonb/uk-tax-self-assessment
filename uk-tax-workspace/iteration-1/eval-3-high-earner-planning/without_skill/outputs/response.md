# Tax Planning for a £105,000 Earner — 2025/26 Tax Year

## Your Situation at a Glance

At £105,000, you face a cluster of tax traps that together create an effective marginal rate far higher than the headline 40% higher-rate band. Understanding each one is the first step to reducing your bill.

---

## The Three Problems You Are Facing

### 1. Personal Allowance Tapering

For the 2025/26 tax year, the standard personal allowance is £12,570. Once your adjusted net income exceeds £100,000, HMRC removes £1 of personal allowance for every £2 of income above that threshold.

At £105,000 your income is £5,000 above the threshold, so you lose £2,500 of your personal allowance. Your effective personal allowance is therefore £12,570 - £2,500 = £10,070.

The income trapped in the taper zone (£100,000 to £125,140) faces an effective marginal rate of 60%:
- 40% higher-rate income tax on the income itself
- Plus the effect of losing £1 of allowance for every £2 earned, which means an additional 40% tax on the lost allowance
- Total: 60% effective marginal rate on every pound earned between £100,000 and £125,140

At £105,000 you are sitting squarely inside this 60% band.

### 2. High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC)

Because your adjusted net income exceeds £60,000 (as of the 2024/25 changes), the High Income Child Benefit Charge applies.

- Between £60,000 and £80,000: you repay 1% of child benefit received for every £200 of income above £60,000
- Above £80,000: you repay 100% of child benefit received

At £105,000, your income is above £80,000, so you must repay the full amount of child benefit received during the tax year via a Self Assessment tax charge. For 2025/26, child benefit rates are approximately:
- £25.60 per week for the eldest/only child
- £16.95 per week for each additional child

With two children that is approximately (£25.60 + £16.95) x 52 = roughly £2,213 per year to repay in full.

### 3. Self Assessment Requirement

Because of the HICBC, you are required to register for and file a Self Assessment tax return each year, even if all your employment income is taxed at source through PAYE.

## Solutions: Reducing Your Adjusted Net Income

The most powerful lever you have is reducing your **adjusted net income** — the figure HMRC uses to calculate both the personal allowance taper and the HICBC. Pension contributions are the most efficient way to do this.

### Option A: Salary Sacrifice (Most Efficient)

Salary sacrifice is the single most tax-efficient option available to you and should be your first choice.

**How it works:** Your employer formally reduces your salary and contributes the difference directly into your pension. Because the contribution never forms part of your pay, it reduces your gross salary for income tax, National Insurance, and adjusted net income purposes.

**The benefits at your income level:**

1. Income tax saving at 60% effective rate (for contributions that bring you from £105,000 down toward £100,000)
2. National Insurance saving — you save employee NI at 2% (the rate above £50,270) on the sacrificed amount
3. Your employer also saves employer NI at 13.8% on the sacrificed amount — many employers pass some or all of this saving back to you as an enhanced employer contribution, effectively giving you free money
4. No need to claim relief on your Self Assessment return — the reduction happens before tax is calculated

**Target:** Sacrifice enough salary to bring your adjusted net income to or below £100,000. At £105,000 that means sacrificing at least £5,000, but you may want to go further depending on your goals (see below).

**Example — sacrificing £5,000:**
- Adjusted net income falls to £100,000
- Personal allowance is fully restored to £12,570
- Tax saving: approximately £5,000 x 60% = £3,000 (income tax alone)
- NI saving: £5,000 x 2% = £100
- You keep your child benefit (~£2,213/year) — no repayment required if you get below £60,000; at £100,000 you still repay in full, but see below

Note: To eliminate the HICBC entirely you need adjusted net income below £60,000. That requires sacrificing £45,000, which may not be practical. To reduce the HICBC, every £200 reduction in income below £80,000 saves 1% of your child benefit.

### Option B: Personal SIPP Contributions

If salary sacrifice is not available or you want to contribute more than your employer allows via sacrifice, you can make personal contributions into your SIPP.

**How it works:**
- You contribute from your net pay; the SIPP provider claims basic-rate tax relief at source (adding 25% to your contribution)
- You claim higher and additional rate relief through Self Assessment
- The gross contribution reduces your adjusted net income for HICBC and personal allowance purposes

**Example — £5,000 net contribution:**
- You pay £5,000 into your SIPP
- The provider claims £1,250 basic-rate relief, making the gross contribution £6,250
- On your Self Assessment return, you claim relief on the difference between 40% and 20% on £6,250 = £1,250 additional tax repayment
- Your adjusted net income is reduced by the gross contribution (£6,250)

**Compared to salary sacrifice:** Personal SIPP contributions do not save National Insurance (neither yours nor your employer's), so salary sacrifice is more efficient if available. However, a SIPP is a good top-up option.

### Option C: Combining Both

You can use salary sacrifice up to the limit your employer allows, then top up with personal SIPP contributions. Together they can bring your adjusted net income down significantly.

## Annual Allowance — How Much Can You Contribute?

Your annual pension allowance for 2025/26 is the lower of:
- **£60,000** (the standard annual allowance)
- **100% of your UK earnings** (i.e., your salary, not including investment income etc.)

At £105,000 salary, the binding limit is £60,000 gross. This includes employer contributions, so coordinate with your employer when planning total contributions.

**Carry forward:** If you have been a member of a registered pension scheme for the previous three tax years and have unused allowance from those years, you can carry that forward and potentially contribute more than £60,000 in a single year. This can be very useful if you want to make a large one-off contribution.

**Tapered annual allowance:** The tapered annual allowance (which reduces the allowance for very high earners) only kicks in when your "threshold income" exceeds £200,000 and your "adjusted income" exceeds £260,000. At £105,000 you are not affected by tapering.

## Practical Steps to Take Now

1. **Talk to your payroll/HR department** about increasing salary sacrifice contributions. Ask whether your employer passes on their NI saving.

2. **Calculate your target.** Decide whether your goal is to:
   - Get below £125,140 (recover any remaining personal allowance)
   - Get below £100,000 (fully restore your personal allowance and save at 60%)
   - Get below £80,000 (start reducing your HICBC repayment)
   - Get below £60,000 (eliminate the HICBC entirely — a saving of ~£2,213/year on top of income tax savings)

3. **Register for Self Assessment** if you have not already done so, given the HICBC obligation. The deadline to register for 2024/25 was 5 October 2025. Penalties apply for late registration.

4. **Consider your carry forward position.** If you have unused pension allowance from 2022/23, 2023/24, and 2024/25, you can potentially make a larger one-off contribution this year to maximise savings.

5. **Keep records of all pension contributions** (employer and personal) so your Self Assessment return is accurate.

## A Note on Your Wife

Your wife has no income and therefore has a personal allowance of £12,570 going unused each year. While transferring income-generating assets to her can be tax-efficient, this is separate from your employment income (you cannot transfer a salary). If you have savings, investments, or rental income, holding these in her name or jointly structured to maximise her allowance can reduce the household tax bill.

The Marriage Allowance also exists (transferring up to £1,260 of unused personal allowance to a basic-rate taxpayer spouse), but it is only available if the higher earner pays tax at the basic rate. As a 40% taxpayer, you do not qualify for Marriage Allowance.
