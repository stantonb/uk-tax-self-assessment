# UK Tax Planning: High Earner at £105,000 (2024/25)

> **DISCLAIMER**: This response provides general tax information only. It is NOT professional tax advice. Tax situations vary — always verify with HMRC guidance or a qualified accountant before making decisions. HMRC is the sole authority on UK tax matters.

---

## Your Situation at a Glance

You earn £105,000, your wife does not work, and you have two children for whom you receive Child Benefit. You face three interconnected tax problems:

1. **Loss of Personal Allowance** — Your income is £5,000 above the £100,000 taper threshold.
2. **High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC)** — Your income is well above the £60,000 HICBC threshold.
3. **High effective marginal rate** — In the £100,000–£125,140 band, your effective marginal rate is approximately 60%, not 40%.

The good news: both your SIPP and your employer's salary sacrifice scheme are powerful tools to address all three problems simultaneously. The key number to target is **£100,000 adjusted net income (ANI)** — bringing your income to or below this threshold eliminates the personal allowance taper entirely and significantly reduces your HICBC charge.

---

## Problem 1: The Personal Allowance Trap

### How the taper works

The standard Personal Allowance for 2024/25 is £12,570. However, for every £2 of ANI above £100,000, you lose £1 of your Personal Allowance.

At £105,000:
- Excess above £100,000 = £5,000
- Personal Allowance lost = £5,000 / 2 = **£2,500**
- Remaining Personal Allowance = £12,570 - £2,500 = **£10,070**

This means you lose £2,500 of tax-free income. That lost allowance is taxed at 40%, costing you an additional **£1,000** in income tax on top of your normal liability.

### Why the effective marginal rate is ~60%

In the £100,000–£125,140 band, each additional £1 of income:
- Is taxed at 40% (higher rate), AND
- Causes you to lose 50p of Personal Allowance, which is also taxed at 40%

Combined effect: 40p tax on the income + 20p tax on the lost allowance = **60p effective tax** per £1 earned. This is the "£100k trap."

### The fix: reduce ANI to £100,000

If you contribute enough to reduce your ANI to exactly £100,000, you restore the full Personal Allowance. The tax saving from that restoration alone is:

- £2,500 Personal Allowance restored x 40% = **£1,000 tax saved**
- Plus 40% relief on the contribution itself

## Problem 2: High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC)

### Your Child Benefit entitlement (2024/25 weekly rates)

| Child | Weekly rate | Annual amount |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| Eldest child | £25.60 | £1,331.20 |
| Second child | £16.95 | £881.40 |
| **Total** | | **£2,212.60** |

### How the HICBC is calculated

The HICBC threshold is **£60,000**. The full charge applies at **£80,000**. Between those points, the charge is 1% of total Child Benefit for every £200 of ANI above £60,000.

At £105,000 ANI, you are above the £80,000 upper limit — meaning **100% of your Child Benefit is clawed back**:

**HICBC = £2,212.60** (the full Child Benefit amount)

### The fix: reduce ANI below £80,000 to reduce the charge; below £60,000 to eliminate it entirely

Bringing ANI to £100,000 still leaves you £40,000 above the HICBC threshold — so you would still owe the full charge. To partially reduce the HICBC, you would need to bring ANI below £80,000. To eliminate it entirely, ANI must fall below £60,000.

However, the most practical and tax-efficient immediate target is **£100,000** — this eliminates the 60% marginal rate zone and restores your full Personal Allowance, which is the most efficient use of pension contributions.

## The Solutions

### Option A: Salary Sacrifice (Recommended First)

**How it works**: Your employer reduces your gross salary in exchange for an increased pension contribution. This reduces your PAYE income — meaning HMRC sees a lower salary, which directly reduces your ANI.

**Why it is more efficient than a personal pension contribution**:

- Salary sacrifice saves **income tax AND National Insurance**
- A personal SIPP contribution saves income tax only (via Self Assessment)

NI saving on salary sacrifice (2024/25 Class 1 rates):
- On earnings between £12,570 and £50,270: 8% NI
- On earnings above £50,270: 2% NI

At £105,000, a £5,000 salary sacrifice saves:
- Income tax: £5,000 x 40% = £2,000 (higher rate relief)
- NI: £5,000 x 2% = £100 (above UEL rate)
- Personal Allowance restored: £2,500 x 40% = £1,000 (as above)
- **Total saving: ~£3,100** on a £5,000 sacrifice

Your employer also saves employer NI (13.8% on the sacrificed amount) — some employers pass this saving on via a higher contribution. It is worth asking.

**Key consideration**: Salary sacrifice reduces your contractual/stated salary, which may affect mortgage affordability calculations, death-in-service cover, and similar salary-linked benefits. Check your employment contract.

---

### Option B: Personal SIPP Contribution

If salary sacrifice is not available or you wish to contribute more than your employer's scheme allows, a personal SIPP contribution also reduces ANI and provides higher-rate tax relief.

**How SIPP relief at source works**:
1. You contribute from net pay — e.g. you pay £4,000 into your SIPP.
2. Your SIPP provider automatically claims basic rate relief (20%) from HMRC, grossing it up to £5,000.
3. You claim the **additional 20% higher-rate relief** via your Self Assessment return.
4. The gross contribution (£5,000) reduces your ANI for HICBC and Personal Allowance purposes.

**Important**: For the SIPP contribution to reduce your ANI, you use the **gross** (total) amount including the basic rate top-up — not just what you paid out of pocket.

**To reduce ANI from £105,000 to £100,000**: You need a gross pension contribution of £5,000. This costs you £3,000 net after basic rate top-up (£5,000 - £1,000 basic rate = £4,000 net contribution, plus the £1,000 added by the provider). Actually more precisely:

- You pay in £4,000
- Provider adds £1,000 basic rate relief = £5,000 gross
- You claim additional £1,000 (20%) via SA = effective net cost £3,000
- ANI reduced by £5,000 — Personal Allowance fully restored
- Tax saving from PA restoration: £1,000
- Net cost after all relief: approximately **£3,000 to achieve £5,000 of pension saving**

---

### Combining Both: The Optimal Strategy

You can use both salary sacrifice and SIPP contributions, as long as your total gross pension contributions (employer + employee + SIPP) do not exceed the **Annual Allowance of £60,000** for 2024/25.

**Recommended priority order**:

1. **Maximise salary sacrifice** up to the amount that reduces your ANI to £100,000 (a reduction of £5,000). This is the most NI-efficient route.
2. **Consider additional SIPP contributions** if you want to further reduce HICBC. Each additional £200 of gross pension contribution reduces HICBC by 1% of your total Child Benefit (i.e., £22.13).
3. **Check carry forward** — if you have unused Annual Allowance from the three previous tax years (2021/22, 2022/23, 2023/24), you can make a larger one-off contribution this year. This could allow a bigger reduction in ANI in one go.

## Important: Your Wife's NI Credits

Your wife is not working and caring for your children. **Do not opt out of Child Benefit payments entirely**, even if you decide to let the HICBC cancel the financial benefit.

Instead, use the **"claim but do not receive payment"** option on the Child Benefit form. This preserves Class 3 NI credits for your wife for each year she is at home with children under 12. These credits count toward her State Pension qualifying years. If she opts out entirely (rather than just declining payment), she loses these credits — which could reduce her State Pension by meaningful amounts over a working lifetime.

## Marriage Allowance: Not Available in Your Case

You may have considered whether Marriage Allowance applies. It does not. Marriage Allowance requires the recipient (you) to be a **basic rate taxpayer only**. As a higher rate taxpayer at £105,000, you are ineligible. Your wife's unused Personal Allowance cannot be transferred to you.

## Self Assessment: You Must File

You are required to file a Self Assessment return because:
- Your ANI exceeds £60,000 and you receive Child Benefit (HICBC liability)
- Your income is above £100,000 (this is a separate SA filing trigger)

If you make SIPP contributions, you must also claim the additional higher-rate relief via SA — it is not applied automatically.

**Deadlines for 2024/25**:
- Register for SA (if not already registered): 5 October 2025
- Online return and payment: **31 January 2026**
- Second payment on account: 31 July 2026

## Immediate Action Checklist

1. **Contact your employer's HR/payroll** — ask about the salary sacrifice pension scheme and how much you can sacrifice. Specifically, ask whether the employer passes on any employer NI savings.

2. **Check your SIPP** — confirm contributions to date for 2024/25 and calculate headroom against the £60,000 Annual Allowance.

3. **Check carry forward** — were you in a pension scheme in 2021/22, 2022/23, and 2023/24? If so, you may have unused Annual Allowance to carry forward for a larger contribution.

4. **Target ANI of £100,000 minimum** — this is the first priority, restoring your full Personal Allowance and ending the 60% marginal rate trap.

5. **Decide how far to go on HICBC** — reducing ANI further reduces (and eventually eliminates) the HICBC, but requires larger pension contributions. Run the maths: reducing HICBC by £2,212 requires reducing ANI by £40,000 (from £100,000 to £60,000).

6. **Do not opt out of Child Benefit entirely** — ensure your wife retains her NI credits.

7. **Register for Self Assessment** if not already registered.

8. **Claim higher-rate pension relief** via your SA return for any SIPP contributions.
