## 2. Capital Gains Tax — Bitcoin (£4,500 gain)

### How HMRC Treats Crypto

HMRC classifies cryptocurrency as property, not currency. Selling Bitcoin is therefore a disposal subject to **Capital Gains Tax (CGT)**, not income tax.

### Your CGT Position

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Bitcoin gain | £4,500 |
| Annual Exempt Amount (2024/25) | £3,000 |
| **Taxable gain** | **£1,500** |

Your gain of £4,500 exceeds the £3,000 annual exempt amount, leaving **£1,500 taxable**.

### CGT Rate — Important Mid-Year Change

The CGT rate on crypto (a non-residential-property asset) **changed on 30 October 2024**:

- **Before 30 October 2024**: Higher-rate taxpayers pay **20%**
- **From 30 October 2024**: Higher-rate taxpayers pay **24%**

Because your employment income of £72,000 already uses up the basic-rate band (which ends at £50,270), **all of your capital gain falls into the higher-rate band**.

The CGT you owe depends on when you sold your Bitcoin:

| Disposal date | Rate | Tax on £1,500 taxable gain |
|---|---|---|
| Before 30 October 2024 | 20% | £300 |
| From 30 October 2024 | 24% | £360 |
| Split across both periods | Blended | Proportionally calculated |

If your sale straddled the October date, the gain must be apportioned between the two periods at the respective rates.

### Share Pooling Rules

When calculating your Bitcoin cost basis, HMRC requires you to apply these matching rules in order:
1. **Same-day rule** — match against any Bitcoin purchased on the same day as the sale
2. **30-day bed and breakfast rule** — match against any Bitcoin purchased within 30 days after the sale
3. **Section 104 pool** — match against pooled average cost of remaining holdings

Make sure your £4,500 gain has been calculated correctly following these rules, not simply as "sale price minus original purchase price" if you have made multiple purchases.

### Record-Keeping

You must retain:
- Date and time of every Bitcoin transaction
- GBP value at time of each transaction
- Exchange records or wallet records
- Bank statements

Keep these for at least 5 years after the 31 January 2026 filing deadline.

## 3. SIP Shares — Held for 3 Years

This is the most nuanced part of your situation. **Please read this section carefully before selling.**

### The Critical 5-Year Rule

SIP shares have a tiered tax treatment based on how long they have been held in the SIP trust:

| Holding period | Income Tax and NI on withdrawal |
|---|---|
| 0 to 3 years | Full market value at withdrawal taxed as employment income |
| **3 to 5 years** | **Lower of: (a) market value at award OR (b) market value at withdrawal — whichever is lower, is taxed** |
| 5 years or more | No Income Tax or NI — full tax relief |

At exactly **3 years**, you fall into the middle tier. This means:

- If your shares have **increased in value** since they were awarded, you will pay Income Tax and NI on the **original award value** (the lower figure).
- If your shares have **decreased in value** since they were awarded, you will pay Income Tax and NI on the **current lower market value**.

### You Are a Higher-Rate Taxpayer

Because you earn £72,000, any employment income charge from withdrawing SIP shares will be taxed at **40% income tax** plus **2% NI** (as you are above the Upper Earnings Limit of £50,270). That is an effective rate of **42%** on the taxable value.

### After Withdrawal — CGT on Further Gains

Once shares leave the SIP trust, they enter your personal ownership. Your CGT base cost becomes the **market value on the date of withdrawal**. Any further gain from that point (if you hold the shares and sell them later at a higher price) is subject to CGT in the normal way.

However, if you sell immediately upon withdrawal, there is no further CGT gain to worry about — the CGT base cost equals the sale price.

### Strong Recommendation: Wait for the 5-Year Mark

If you can wait until your shares have been in the SIP for **5 full years**, you will pay **no Income Tax and no NI** on the full value. Given you are only 2 years away from that milestone, the tax savings could be substantial — especially at your 42% combined rate.

**Example**: If your SIP shares are worth £10,000 at the time of withdrawal:
- Selling now (3-year mark): up to £4,200 in Income Tax and NI (42% of £10,000 or the award value, whichever is lower)
- Waiting until 5 years: £0 Income Tax and NI on the same £10,000

### What to Check

Before proceeding, confirm:
1. The exact date the shares were awarded (to establish whether you have truly reached 3 years)
2. The market value at the original award date
3. The current market value
4. Whether any of the shares are Free Shares, Partnership Shares, or Matching Shares (all follow the same 5-year rule)

## 5. Self Assessment — Do You Need to File?

**Yes — you must file a Self Assessment return for 2024/25.**

You are required to file because your Bitcoin capital gain of £4,500 **exceeds the £3,000 annual exempt amount**. This is one of the specific triggers for SA.

Additionally, if your SIP shares are withdrawn mid-year and the Income Tax/NI is not fully handled through PAYE (unlikely but worth checking), this could create an additional SA obligation.

### Your SA Reporting Requirements

On your 2024/25 Self Assessment return (SA100), you will need to complete:

- **Employment pages (SA102)** — report your P60 employment income and PAYE tax paid
- **Capital Gains Tax summary pages (SA108)** — report your Bitcoin disposal:
  - Proceeds from sale
  - Cost basis (purchase price plus any fees, calculated using pooling rules)
  - Net gain of £4,500
  - Annual exempt amount of £3,000
  - Taxable gain of £1,500
- **Student loan section** — indicate Plan 2; HMRC will reconcile against PAYE deductions already made
- **SIP shares** — if you withdraw them this tax year, any Income Tax/NI charge should appear via your employer's PAYE. Verify it is on your payslip or P60. Any subsequent CGT on disposal after withdrawal goes on SA108.

| Paper return | 31 October 2025 | If filing on paper |

**Today's date is 24 March 2026.** If you have not yet filed your 2024/25 return, the **31 January 2026 online deadline has already passed**.
