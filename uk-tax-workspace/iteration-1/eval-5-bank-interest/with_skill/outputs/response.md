## Step 2: Do banks "sort out the tax automatically"?

Since April 2016, banks and building societies **no longer deduct tax at source**. All interest is now paid gross — meaning no tax is taken before it reaches you. So the old idea that "the bank deals with it" no longer applies.

What does happen is that your bank reports your interest figures to HMRC directly. For smaller amounts, HMRC may collect any tax owed by adjusting your PAYE tax code (reducing your tax-free pay to recover the amount). However, this is an administrative convenience — it does not mean you have no obligation to report or that the tax is automatically handled correctly.

## Step 3: What is your Personal Savings Allowance (PSA)?

Your PSA depends on your income tax band.

Your employment income is **£48,000**. Adding your taxable savings interest of £1,600 gives total income of **£49,600**.

- The higher rate threshold is £50,270.
- At £49,600, you remain a **basic rate taxpayer** (just under the higher rate threshold).

As a basic rate taxpayer, your Personal Savings Allowance is **£1,000**.

This means the first £1,000 of your savings interest each year is taxed at 0%.

---

## Step 4: The starting rate for savings — does it apply to you?

The starting rate for savings (0% on up to £5,000 of savings interest) is only available to people whose non-savings income is low. Specifically, it is reduced by £1 for every £1 of non-savings income above the personal allowance.

Your employment income is £48,000, which is well above the personal allowance of £12,570. The starting rate band is therefore **fully used up** and unavailable to you. This allowance is designed for retirees or people with very low earned income — it does not apply in your situation.

## Step 6: Do you need to file a Self Assessment return?

Your savings interest (£1,600) **exceeds your PSA (£1,000)**. This is one of the triggers for Self Assessment under HMRC's rules.

However, whether you actually need to file a full SA return — rather than HMRC simply adjusting your tax code — depends on a few factors:

- HMRC receives interest data directly from banks and NS&I. For modest amounts like yours, they often collect the tax by issuing a revised tax code (which reduces your personal allowance through PAYE) rather than requiring a formal return.
- If you do not already file a Self Assessment return for another reason, HMRC may handle this via a Simple Assessment letter or a tax code adjustment, and you would not need to file an SA100.
- If HMRC issues you a notice to file (SA316), however, filing becomes **mandatory regardless**.

**In practice:** You should not simply do nothing. The safest approach is to check whether HMRC has already adjusted your tax code to collect this tax. If they have not, and you do not receive a notice to file, you should contact HMRC to ensure the underpayment is collected. If you have any doubt, or if you have other reasons to file (such as needing to claim a relief or reporting other untaxed income), filing a return is the cleanest solution.
