# UK Tax Guide: Nurse with Online Tutoring Side Business

## Your Situation at a Glance

- **Employment income:** £35,000 (nurse, PAYE)
- **Tutoring revenue:** £3,200 (tax year 2024/25, started September 2024)
- **Business expenses claimed:** £5,500 (laptop, webcam, software, advertising)
- **Apparent trading loss:** £2,300 (£3,200 - £5,500)
- **Working from:** rented flat

---

## 1. Do You Need to File a Self Assessment Tax Return?

**Yes, almost certainly.** You must register for Self Assessment and file a tax return because:

- You have self-employment income from your tutoring business.
- HMRC requires you to register if your self-employment income exceeds **£1,000** in a tax year (the Trading Allowance threshold).

Since your revenue is £3,200 — well above £1,000 — you must register.

**Deadline to register:** You should have registered by **5 October 2025** for the 2024/25 tax year (the tax year runs 6 April 2024 to 5 April 2025). If you have not done so yet, register as soon as possible to avoid a penalty. You can register online at HMRC's website.

**Tax return deadline:**
- Paper return: 31 October 2025
- Online return: **31 January 2026**

If you are reading this close to or after those dates, file immediately — late filing penalties start at £100.

---

## 2. The Trading Allowance — Should You Use It?

HMRC offers a **£1,000 Trading Allowance**. If your gross self-employment income is £1,000 or less, you pay no tax and don't need to report it. However, since you earned £3,200, the allowance does not exempt you from filing.

You can choose to use the trading allowance instead of claiming actual expenses, but this would mean:
- Taxable profit = £3,200 - £1,000 = £2,200 (a taxable profit)

This is **worse** than claiming your actual expenses (which produce a loss), so you should **not** use the trading allowance in your case. Claim your actual expenses.

---

## 3. Your Business Expenses — What You Can Claim

To be deductible, expenses must be "wholly and exclusively" for the purpose of your business. Here is how your £5,500 breaks down:

### Laptop (capital expenditure)
A laptop is a **capital asset**, not a revenue expense. However, HMRC's **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA)** and **100% First Year Allowances** allow most small businesses to deduct the full cost of equipment in the year of purchase through **Capital Allowances**.

Under the **Full Expensing / AIA rules**, you can claim the full cost of the laptop in year one, provided it is used exclusively for business. If there is personal use, you must apportion — for example, if you use the laptop 70% for business and 30% personally, you can only claim 70% of the cost.

**Practical point:** Be honest about personal use. HMRC may challenge a claim for 100% business use of a laptop that is also the only computer in your home.

### Webcam
Same treatment as the laptop — a capital item, claimable in full under AIA/First Year Allowances, subject to any personal use apportionment.

### Software Subscriptions
If these are recurring subscriptions (e.g., monthly/annual fees for Zoom, tutoring platforms, scheduling tools), these are **revenue expenses** deductible in full, provided they are wholly for business use. If any software is also used personally, apportion accordingly.

### Advertising
Fully deductible as a revenue expense — for example, paid social media ads, Google Ads, directory listings related to your tutoring business.

## 4. Can You Claim Rent and Household Bills? (Use of Home)

This is a common question and the answer is: **yes, partially, but with important caveats.**

### The Rule: "Wholly and Exclusively" — with a Twist for Home Use

You **cannot** claim your full rent or bills because these costs are not incurred "wholly and exclusively" for business. However, HMRC does allow a **proportionate deduction** for the business use of your home.

### Method 1: HMRC Flat Rate (Simplified Expenses)
HMRC offers a simplified flat rate that avoids complex calculations:

| Hours of business use per month | Flat rate per month |
|----------------------------------|---------------------|
| 25–50 hours                      | £10                 |
| 51–100 hours                     | £18                 |
| 101+ hours                       | £26                 |

For example, if you tutor 60 hours a month from home, you could claim £18/month x 7 months (September 2024 to April 2025) = **£126**.

This is simple but may understate your actual costs.

### Method 2: Actual Cost Apportionment
You can calculate the actual proportion of your home used for business and claim that share of relevant costs. The calculation typically uses:

- **Number of rooms:** If your flat has 4 rooms and you use 1 for tutoring, that is 25% of the property.
- **Time:** If you use that room for business 4 hours out of 24 hours per day, that is a further time apportionment.

**Example:**
- Annual rent: £12,000
- Annual bills (heating, electricity, broadband): £2,400
- Total: £14,400
- Room proportion: 1 out of 4 rooms = 25%
- Daily time proportion: 4 hours/day business use = 4/24 = 16.7%
- Claimable: £14,400 x 25% x 16.7% ≈ **£600/year**, or proportionately for the months in business.

**Important caveat — rented property:**
If you are renting, claiming a portion of rent for business use is generally fine for tax purposes and does **not** automatically make you liable for business rates (unlike if you own your home and exclusively use a room for business). However, you should check your tenancy agreement, as some landlords prohibit running a business from the property.

**Broadband:** If you already pay for broadband and it is used for both personal and business purposes, you can claim a reasonable business proportion (e.g., 50% if roughly half your usage is business-related). You cannot claim the full cost.

### What You Cannot Claim
- Fixed costs that are the same regardless of business use (e.g., your full rent) — only the business proportion.
- Mortgage capital repayments (not applicable as you rent).
- Council Tax — this is specifically excluded by HMRC for home-working deductions by the self-employed (unlike employees who work from home).

## 5. Your Trading Loss — What Happens to the £2,300?

After accounting for your expenses properly, let us say your loss is confirmed at approximately £2,300 (and potentially a little more if you add a home use claim). This is a **trading loss** from self-employment.

### Options for Using Your Loss

#### Option A: Offset Against Other Income in the Same Tax Year (Most Beneficial)
Under **s64 ITA 2007**, you can set a trading loss from self-employment against your **other income in the same tax year** — which includes your PAYE nursing salary of £35,000.

This means:
- Your taxable employment income effectively reduces by the amount of the loss.
- You will get a tax refund (or reduction) from HMRC.

**Example:**
- Employment income: £35,000
- Trading loss: £2,300
- Net taxable income: £32,700
- Tax saving at 20% basic rate: approximately **£460**
- Plus NI considerations may add a small further saving.

You make this claim on your Self Assessment tax return (SA100, supplementary page SA103S for self-employment).

#### Option B: Carry the Loss Forward
If you do not claim the loss against 2024/25 income, you can **carry it forward** to offset against future profits from the **same business**. This is less useful if you expect losses to continue or if you want cash now.

#### Option C: Carry Back
You can also carry a loss back one year against other income in 2023/24, though this requires an amendment to a previous return (if you filed one) and is only relevant if you had significant other income in that year.

### "Hobby" vs. "Trade" — A Risk to Be Aware Of

HMRC may challenge whether your tutoring is a genuine trade if losses are sustained over multiple years with no realistic prospect of profit. Key factors HMRC considers (the "badges of trade"):
- Do you intend to make a profit?
- Is the activity carried out in a businesslike manner?
- Do you have relevant skills or qualifications?

As a nurse starting a tutoring business, you clearly have skills and a business rationale. Keep records of your intention to grow the business. One year of losses is not unusual for a startup and is unlikely to attract scrutiny.

## 6. National Insurance Contributions (NICs)

As a self-employed person, you may also owe **Class 4 NICs** on profits, and possibly **Class 2 NICs**.

- Since you have made a **loss**, you owe **no Class 4 NICs** (these apply only to profits above £12,570).
- **Class 2 NICs** (a flat rate of £3.45/week in 2024/25) apply if your profits exceed the Small Profits Threshold of **£6,725**. Since you have a loss, you are below this threshold and owe no Class 2 NICs.
- However, you may want to pay **voluntary Class 2 NICs** to protect your State Pension entitlement if this tax year would otherwise be a gap year. This costs very little (£3.45/week) and can be worthwhile.

---

## 7. VAT

With £3,200 in revenue, you are far below the VAT registration threshold (£90,000 for 2024/25). You do **not** need to register for VAT and do not need to worry about this.

---

## 8. Records You Must Keep

HMRC requires you to keep business records for at least **5 years after the 31 January submission deadline** for that tax year. Keep:

- Invoices or records of all tutoring income (dates, amounts, students)
- Receipts for all expenses (laptop, webcam, software subscriptions, advertising)
- Bank statements
- Records of business hours worked from home (to support any home-use claim)
- Any contracts or agreements with students

## 9. Step-by-Step Action Plan

1. **Register for Self Assessment** at HMRC.gov.uk if you have not already done so. Call HMRC on 0300 200 3500 if you are past the October 2025 deadline — register and file as soon as possible.

2. **Gather all income records** — total tutoring revenue received between September 2024 and 5 April 2025.

3. **Gather all expense receipts** — laptop, webcam, software, advertising. Note the purchase dates and check for any personal use that requires apportionment.

4. **Calculate home use** — decide whether to use the flat rate or actual cost method. The flat rate is simpler; the actual cost method may yield a higher deduction.

5. **Complete your SA100 and SA103S** (short self-employment supplementary page, appropriate for turnover under £85,000) online via your HMRC Government Gateway account.

6. **Claim your loss against employment income** on the return to get a tax refund.

7. **Submit by 31 January 2026** (online deadline). Pay any tax due by the same date (in your case you are likely to receive a refund, not owe tax).

8. **Consider a simple bookkeeping system** going forward — even a spreadsheet — to make next year's return easier.
