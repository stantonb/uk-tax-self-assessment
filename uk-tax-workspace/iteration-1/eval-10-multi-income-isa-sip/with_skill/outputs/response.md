## Step 1: Establishing Your Tax Band

Your salary of £67,000 puts you firmly in the **higher rate band** (40%). This is critical because it affects the allowances that apply to your savings interest and dividends.

The personal allowance of £12,570 is applied against your salary first, giving taxable employment income of **£54,430**. Your salary alone exceeds the higher rate threshold of £50,270, so all of your investment income (savings and dividends) sits on top of income already in the higher rate band.

## Step 2: Share Incentive Plan (SIP) — Nothing to Declare (Yet)

This is the good news. Both your SIP components are fully sheltered for now.

**Free shares (£3,600):** Your employer awarded you £3,600 in free shares, which is exactly at the annual limit for free shares (£3,600 per year). Because these shares remain in the SIP trust and you have not withdrawn them, there is **no Income Tax and no National Insurance due now**. Nothing goes on your tax return for these.

**Partnership shares (£1,800):** You purchased these from your gross salary — meaning Income Tax and NI were never deducted on that £1,800 in the first place. You have already received the tax relief automatically through payroll. Again, nothing to report on your return while the shares remain in the plan.

**The critical 5-year rule:** If you withdraw the shares before holding them for 5 years, an Income Tax and NI charge will apply. After 5 years, withdrawal is completely free of Income Tax and NI. Any gain after you withdraw the shares into personal ownership becomes subject to CGT, using the market value on withdrawal date as your CGT base cost.

**Action:** Keep the shares in the SIP trust for at least 5 years from the date of award to secure the full tax relief. Do not withdraw early without calculating the income tax cost first.

## Step 6: Capital Gains on the Shares Sold (£2,800 gain)

You sold shares that you held before moving to the UK, realising a gain of approximately £2,800.

The **CGT annual exempt amount for 2024/25 is £3,000**. Your gain of £2,800 falls entirely within this exempt amount.

**Result: No CGT is due on this disposal.**

However, you still need to check whether you must complete the SA108 Capital Gains Tax summary pages. The reporting requirement is triggered if your total disposal proceeds exceed four times the annual exempt amount (i.e., four times £3,000 = £12,000). If your total proceeds from selling the shares were below £12,000, you do not need to complete SA108. If proceeds exceeded £12,000, you must complete it even though no tax is due.

**Important note on overseas gains:** You mention these shares were from before you moved to the UK. The gain is calculated on proceeds minus your original acquisition cost. For shares acquired in a foreign currency, you will need to convert both the acquisition cost and disposal proceeds to sterling using HMRC-approved exchange rates for the relevant dates. If the shares were gifted to you or inherited, different base cost rules apply. Clarify the acquisition circumstances with an accountant if in any doubt.

**CGT rates note:** For 2024/25, CGT rates on shares changed mid-year. Gains on shares disposed of before 30 October 2024 are taxed at 10% (basic rate) or 20% (higher rate). Gains on shares disposed of on or after 30 October 2024 are taxed at 18% (basic rate) or 24% (higher rate). As a higher rate taxpayer, the rate that would have applied if your gain exceeded the exempt amount depends on the disposal date — but since your £2,800 gain is within the £3,000 exempt amount, this does not affect you this year.

## Step 7: Postgraduate Loan Repayments

Your postgraduate loan repayments are calculated separately from any undergraduate student loan plan.

- **Postgraduate loan threshold:** £21,000 per year
- **Repayment rate:** 6% of income above £21,000

Your relevant income for student loan purposes includes your total income (salary plus taxable investment income). Using your salary of £67,000 as the main figure:

- Income above threshold: £67,000 − £21,000 = £46,000
- Repayment: 6% × £46,000 = **£2,760 per year**

Your employer should already be deducting postgraduate loan repayments through PAYE if HMRC has issued a PGL1 notice to your employer. Check your payslips to confirm deductions are being made.

On your Self Assessment return, you must declare your postgraduate loan in the student loan section. HMRC will calculate the total repayment due on all income and offset any PAYE deductions already made. If your employer has been deducting correctly on your salary throughout the year, there may be little or no additional repayment due through SA — but SA is still required to reconcile the position across all income sources (including dividends and savings interest above the threshold).

Note: Postgraduate loan repayments are **not** included in payments on account. The full annual liability is settled as a balancing payment by 31 January.

## Are You Being Tax Efficient?

You are making good use of some allowances, but there are concrete steps that could improve your position.

### What you are already doing well

**SIP — excellent use of the scheme.** Buying £1,800 in partnership shares from gross salary saves you 40% Income Tax plus 2% NI (above UEL) on that amount — roughly £756 in tax saved. The £3,600 in free shares is completely tax-sheltered while in the plan. Keep both for 5 years.

**ISA — good, but not maximised.** Investing £10,000 in your Stocks and Shares ISA is sensible. All future growth and income from those investments will be permanently tax-free.

### Where you can improve

**1. Use your remaining ISA allowance (£10,000 unused)**

You have £10,000 of the £20,000 annual ISA allowance remaining. Consider a **bed and ISA strategy** on your general investment account (GIA) holdings:

- Sell shares in your GIA that have gains within the £3,000 CGT annual exempt amount
- Immediately rebuy the same shares inside your Stocks and Shares ISA
- The 30-day bed and breakfast rule does not apply when rebuying inside an ISA
- Future dividends and gains on those holdings will be permanently sheltered from tax

Given that you are currently paying 33.75% on GIA dividends above your allowance, moving high-dividend holdings into your ISA is particularly valuable.

**2. Consider a pension contribution to reduce adjusted net income**

At £67,000, you are well within the higher rate band. Every £1 of additional pension contribution saves you 40p in Income Tax (and potentially more through employer salary sacrifice). If your employer offers salary sacrifice for pension, this also saves 2% NI on contributions.

While your income of £67,000 is not near the £100,000 personal allowance taper, a pension contribution:
- Reduces your adjusted net income, which is the figure used to determine your PSA and dividend tax
- If a pension contribution brought your non-savings income back into the basic rate band, your savings and dividends would attract lower tax rates (though at £67,000 this would require a very substantial contribution)

**3. Your dividends above the allowance cost £978.75 — shelter them**

The £2,900 taxable dividend bill is the largest avoidable item here. Moving dividend-paying holdings from your GIA into your ISA over coming years (using the bed and ISA technique each year within your annual CGT exempt amount) will progressively eliminate this charge.

**4. Check your employer deductions**

Verify on your payslips that:
- SIP partnership share deductions are shown as pre-tax (reducing your taxable pay)
- Postgraduate loan repayments are being deducted (PGL1 code applied by employer)
- Your tax code is correct — HMRC may have already incorporated some investment income adjustments

**5. Record keeping for the SIP 5-year clock**

Note the exact award dates of both your free shares and your partnership shares. The 5-year clock runs from each award/purchase date. If you receive SIP shares in future years, each tranche has its own 5-year period. Keep the SIP plan documentation from your employer.
